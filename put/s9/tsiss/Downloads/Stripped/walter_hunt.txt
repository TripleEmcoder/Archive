   Walter Hunt (1796–1859) was an [21]American mechanic. He lived and
   worked in [22]New York state. Through the course of his work he became
   renowned for being a prolific inventor, notably of the [23]sewing
   machine (1833), [24]safety pin (1849), a forerunner of the
   [25]Winchester repeating rifle, a successful [26]flax spinner, knife
   sharpener, [27]streetcar [28]bell, hard-[29]coal-burning [30]stove,
   artificial stone, [31]street sweeping machinery, the [32]velocipede,
   and the [33]ice plough^[34][1].

   Walter Hunt did not realize the significance of many of these when he
   invented them; today, many are widely-used products. He thought little
   of the safety pin, selling the patent for a paltry sum of $400 (roughly
   $10,000 in 2008 dollars)^[35][2] to the company [36]W R Grace and
   Company, to pay a man to whom he owed $15. He failed to patent his
   sewing machine at all, because he feared that it would create
   [37]unemployment among seamstresses. (This led to an 1854 court case
   when the machine was re-invented by [38]Elias Howe; Hunt's machine
   shown to have design flaws limiting its practical use). In seeking
   patents for his inventions, Hunt used the services of [39]Charles
   Grafton Page, a patent solicitor who had previously worked at the
   [40]US Patent Office. Like Howe, Hunt is buried in [41]Green-Wood
   Cemetery in [42]Brooklyn, New York.
