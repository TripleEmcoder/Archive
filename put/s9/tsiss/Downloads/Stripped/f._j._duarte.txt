   F. J. Duarte is a [30]laser physicist and author/editor of several
   well-known books on [31]tunable
   lasers.^[32][1]^[33][2]^[34][3]^[35][4]^[36][5]^[37][6]^[38][7] He
   introduced the generalized [39]multiple-prism dispersion
   theory^[40][8]^[41][9]^[42][10] and has discovered various
   multiple-prism grating oscillator laser configurations.^[43][11] These
   configurations include the multiple-prism near-grazing-incidence
   grating cavities originally disclosed as [44]copper-laser-pumped
   narrow-linewidth tunable lasers.^[45][12]^[46][13] Duarte's
   contributions have found applications in a variety of fields including:
     * [47]atomic vapor laser isotope
       separation,^[48][14]^[49][15]^[50][16]
     * [51]laser medicine,^[52][17]^[53][18]^[54][19]
     * laser [55]microscopy,^[56][20]^[57][21]
     * [58]laser pulse compression,^[59][22]^[60][23]^[61][24]
     * [62]laser spectroscopy,^[63][25]^[64][26]
     * tunable [65]diode lasers.^[66][27]^[67][28]

[[75]edit] Solid state dye laser development

   From the mid 1980s to early 1990s Duarte and scientists from the [76]US
   Army Missile Command developed, and demonstrated, ruggedized
   [77]narrow-linewidth laser oscillators tunable directly in the visible
   spectrum.^[78][29]^[79][30] This pioneering work led to experimentation
   with polymer gain media and in 1994 Duarte reported on the first
   narrow-linewidth tunable [80]solid state dye laser
   oscillators.^[81][31] These dispersive oscillator architectures were
   then refined to yield single-longitudinal-mode emission limited only by
   [82]Heisenberg's uncertainty principle.^[83][32] His work on
   solid-state organic-inorganic materials led to the assessment of
   intra-gain-medium interference phenomena and to the emission of
   low-divergence homogeneous laser beams from polymer-nanoparticle gain
   media.^[84][33] In 2005 Duarte and colleagues were the first to
   demonstrate coherent emission from an electrically excited [85]organic
   semiconductor.^[86][34]^[87][35]

[[88]edit] Tunable lasers for the nuclear energy industry

   As previously outlined, Duarte and Piper developed narrow-linewidth
   [89]multiple-prism grating laser oscillators^[90][13]^[91][36] whose
   designs have been adopted by various research groups working on
   [92]uranium [93]atomic vapor laser isotope separation
   (AVLIS).^[94][14]^[95][15]^[96][16] This work^[97][13]^[98][36] was
   supported by the [99]Australian Atomic Energy Commission.^[100][36] In
   a review article,^[101][37] Duarte discloses that during the course of
   this research^[102][13]^[103][36] he did approach the then federal
   minister for energy, [104]Sir John L. Carrick, to advocate for the
   introduction of an AVLIS facility in Australia. In 2002, he
   participated in research that led to the isotope separation of
   [105]lithium using tunable diode lasers.^[106][38]

[[107]edit] Interferometry

   In the late 1980s, Duarte applied [108]Dirac’s notation to describe
   quantum mechanically the [109]N-slit interferometer and to solve
   problems in [110]industrial imaging and [111]optical
   metrology.^[112][39]^[113][40]^[114][41] The electro-optical N-slit
   interferometer uses prismatic [115]beam expansion to illuminate the
   N-slit array and comprises a photodiode array, at the interference
   plane, to register the interferograms.^[116][42] This work also led to
   a generalized [117]N-slit interferometric equation that was then
   applied to describe [118]interference, [119]diffraction,
   [120]refraction, and [121]reflection, in a rational and unified
   approach.^[122][43]^[123][44]

[[124]edit] Macquarie University

   Duarte studied physics at [125]Macquarie University ([126]Sydney,
   Australia) where he also established the successful [127]science reform
   movement of the late 1970s.^[128][45]^[129][46] Science reform, at
   Macquarie, was widely supported by local scientists including
   physicists [130]Ronald Ernest Aitchison, [131]R. E. B. Makinson, and
   the famed [132]John Clive Ward.^[133][47] His career path includes
   post-doctoral research in Australia, plus academic and
   industrial-research appointments in the [134]United States.
