   Edward Joseph Hoffman, ([14]1942 - July 1, 2004) helped invent the
   first human [15]PET scanner, a commonly used [16]whole-body scanning
   procedure for detecting diseases like [17]cancer. Hoffman, with
   [18]Michael Phelps, developed the [19]Positron Emission Tomography
   scanner in 1973.

   Hoffman was born in St. Louis, Missouri. He earned a BS chemistry from
   [20]St. Louis University in 1963, his Ph.D. in Nuclear Chemistry from
   [21]Washington University in St. Louis in 1970 and completed
   postgraduate work in nuclear chemistry at the [22]University of
   Pennsylvania. In 1972 he joined the faculty of Washington University’s
   School of Medicine, where he and Dr. Michael Phelps began developing
   what later became known as the PET scanner, used to detect cancer,
   heart disease and other serious illnesses. In 1975 , Phelps and Hoffman
   moved to the University of Pennsylvania.^[23][1]

   Starting in 1976, Hoffman was a professor at [24]UCLA medical school in
   the Departments of Molecular and Medical Pharmacology and Radiological
   Sciences.^[25][2] In 1999, he authored a book that reviews the
   biochemical basis of alternative medical treatments for
   cancer.^[26][3]^[27][4] He served as Editor-in-Chief of the journal
   [28]IEEE Transactions on Nuclear Science.^[29][5]

   He died of liver cancer in [30]Los Angeles, [31]California.
