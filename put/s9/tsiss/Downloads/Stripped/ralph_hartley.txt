   Ralph Vinton Lyon Hartley (November 30, 1888 – May 1, 1970) was an
   [16]electronics researcher. He invented the [17]Hartley oscillator and
   the [18]Hartley transform, and contributed to the foundations of
   [19]information theory.

[[25]edit] Biography

   Hartley was born in [26]Spruce, Nevada, [27]USA and attended the
   [28]University of Utah, receiving an A.B. degree in 1909. He became a
   [29]Rhodes Scholar at [30]St Johns, [31]Oxford University, in 1910 and
   received a B.A. degree in 1912 and a B.Sc. degree in 1913. He married
   Florence Vail of [32]Brooklyn on March 21, 1916.^[33][1] The Hartleys
   had no children.

   He returned to the [34]United States and was employed at the Research
   Laboratory of the [35]Western Electric Company. In 1915 he was in
   charge of [36]radio receiver development for the [37]Bell System
   transatlantic [38]radiotelephone tests. For this he developed the
   Hartley oscillator and also a neutralizing circuit to eliminate triode
   singing resulting from internal coupling. A [39]patent for the
   oscillator was filed on June 1, 1915 and awarded on October 26, 1920.

   During [40]World War I he established the principles that led to
   sound-type [41]directional finders.

   Following the war he returned to [42]Western Electric. He later worked
   at [43]Bell Laboratories. He performed research on [44]repeaters and
   voice and carrier transmission and formulated the law "that the total
   amount of information that can be transmitted is proportional to
   frequency range transmitted and the time of the transmission." After
   about 10 years of illness he returned to Bell Labs in 1939 as a
   [45]consultant.

   During [46]World War II he was particularly involved with
   [47]servomechanism problems.

   He retired from Bell Labs in 1950 and died on May 1, 1970.

[[48]edit] Awards

     * [49]IRE Medal of Honor, 1946, for his oscillator and information
       proportionality law. This was an award from the [50]Institute of
       Radio Engineers which later merged into the [51]Institute of
       Electrical and Electronics Engineers; the award became the [52]IEEE
       Medal of Honor.
     * Fellow of the [53]American Association for the Advancement of
       Science
