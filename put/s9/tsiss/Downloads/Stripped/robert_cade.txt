   James Robert Cade (September 26, 1927 – November 27, 2007) was an
   [17]American [18]physician, [19]university professor, [20]research
   scientist and [21]inventor. Cade, a native of [22]Texas, earned his
   [23]undergraduate and [24]medical degrees, and became a professor of
   medicine and [25]nephrology at the [26]University of Florida located in
   [27]Gainesville, Florida. Although Cade engaged in many areas of
   medical research, he is widely remembered as the leader of the research
   team that formulated the sports drink [28]Gatorade.^[29][1]

[[37]edit] Early life and education

   Robert Cade was born in [38]San Antonio, Texas on September 26,
   1927.^[39][2] Cade took an early interest in athletics, and ran the
   mile in four minutes, twenty seconds in [40]high school,^[41][2] a very
   respectable time for a high school athlete in the early 1940s.^[42][3]
   He served in the [43]U.S. Navy during the last year of [44]World War
   II, and studied at the [45]University of Texas after being
   discharged.^[46][4] While in college, he joined [47]Delta Upsilon
   [48]fraternity and earned his [49]bachelor of science degree. In 1953,
   he married Mary Strasburger, a nurse from [50]Dallas, Texas.^[51][5]
   After graduating with his [52]doctor of medicine degree from the
   University of Texas [53]Southwestern Medical School in Dallas in 1954,
   Cade interned at the Saint Louis City Hospital in [54]Saint Louis,
   Missouri and did his residency at [55]Parkland Memorial Hospital in
   Dallas.^[56][4] He also served fellowships at his alma mater,
   Southwestern Medical School, and [57]Cornell University [58]Medical
   College in [59]New York, New York.^[60][4] Cade joined the faculty of
   the University of Florida [61]College of Medicine in 1961 as an
   assistant professor in its [62]renal division.^[63][2]

[[64]edit] Invention of Gatorade

   In 1965, Cade was approached by Dewayne Douglas, an assistant coach for
   the [65]Florida Gators football team, about the extreme [66]dehydration
   faced by Gator football players practicing in the high temperatures and
   [67]humidity of the [68]Deep South in late summer and early
   fall.^[69][6] Douglas questioned Cade why his football players did not
   [70]urinate during practice and games.^[71][6] Cade learned from
   anecdotal evidence that football players were losing water through
   [72]perspiration and failing to replace fluid during practice and
   games.^[73][6] Cade's research team discovered that football players
   were losing up to 18 pounds (8.2 kg) during the three hours of a
   [74]college football game, and that ninety to ninety-five percent of
   that loss was water.^[75][4] A player's [76]plasma volume could
   decrease as much as seven percent and blood volume by five percent, and
   sodium and chloride were excreted in the sweat.^[77][4]

   During 1965 and 1966, Cade, together with his team of research doctors
   Dana Shires, James Free, and Alejandro M. de Quesada, conducted a
   series of trial-and-error experiments with his
   [78]glucose-and-[79]electrolytes rehydration drink on members of the
   Gators football team of coach [80]Ray Graves, first with members of the
   [81]freshman squad, and after initially promising results, with
   starting members of the [82]varsity team.^[83][1] "It didn't taste like
   Gatorade," Cade said in a 1988 interview with [84]Florida Trend
   magazine.^[85][2] In fact, according to Cade, when one Gator football
   player tried it, he spat it out and strongly suggested that the
   original experimental formula tasted more like bodily waste.^[86][2]
   Dana Shires remembered that "it sort of tasted like toilet bowl
   cleaner."^[87][7] To make it more palatable, at the suggestion of
   Cade's wife, the researchers added lemon juice and
   [88]cyclamate^[89][8] to the original formula of water, [90]salt,
   [91]sodium citrate, [92]fructose and [93]monopotassium
   phosphate.^[94][7]

   Cade appeared in "The Legend of Gatorade" television commercials
   narrated by long-time college football announcer [95]Keith Jackson in
   2005,^[96][9] during which Cade declared, "Naturally, we called our
   stuff Gatorade."^[97][10] However, the rehydration drink was first
   known as "Cade's Ade"^[98][11] and "Cade's Cola" to the Florida Gators
   football team, and only later became known as "Gatorade."^[99][7] The
   drink received its first real test in the Gators' 1965 game against the
   [100]LSU Tigers football team; the Tigers faded in the 102 °F (39 °C)
   heat of the second half and the Gators did not.^[101][2] Coach Graves
   was convinced, and asked Cade to produce enough of his potion for all
   Gator games. Gatorade achieved national notoriety as a result of the
   Gators' first [102]Orange Bowl title over the [103]Georgia Tech Yellow
   Jackets in January 1967.^[104][7] The Gators reinforced their
   reputation as a "second-half team" and came from behind to defeat the
   Yellow Jackets 27–10.^[105][12] Afterward, Georgia Tech coach [106]Bud
   Carson told reporters: "We didn't have Gatorade; that made the
   difference."^[107][7]

   Cade [108]patented the formula and offered all of the rights to the
   drink to the University of Florida in exchange for the university's
   backing of the production and marketing of the drink, but the
   university turned down his proposal.^[109][13] He initially obtained
   bank financing and began to produce "Gatorade" through his own
   business, but later entered into a contract with [110]Stokely-Van Camp,
   Inc. to produce and sell the drink.^[111][8] When sales [112]royalties
   reached $200,000, the university took notice.^[113][14] The
   [114]Florida Board of Regents, prompted by the [115]U.S. Department of
   Health, Education and Welfare, which had provided Cade with a small
   grant for his research, asked for the patent rights.^[116][15] Cade
   refused.^[117][14] The Board of Regents, acting on behalf of the
   university, then brought suit against Cade for a share of the
   profits,^[118][15] arguing that the university's facilities, employees
   and students were instrumental in the development of the
   product.^[119][14]^[120][16] After thirty-one months of legal
   wrangling, Cade and the university negotiated a settlement of their
   dispute in 1972,^[121][14] and the Board of the Regents and the
   university settled for a twenty percent share of the
   royalties.^[122][16] Cade, and his investors in the Gatorade Trust,
   retained eighty percent. In the aftermath of the settlement, all
   parties decided to play nice—of the first $70,500 in Gatorade royalties
   received by the university, the university reinvested $30,000 in kidney
   research by Cade's renal department and another $12,000 in Cade's other
   research projects.^[123][17] Cade, for his part, created multiple
   scholarships and contributed generously to the university from his own
   royalties over the following years.

[[124]edit] Legacy

   Through 2007, the latest year for which figures are publicly available,
   the University of Florida has realized over $110 million from its share
   of the Gatorade royalties.^[125][14]^[126][18] Cade and his associates'
   share of the royalties is undisclosed, the majority of their rights
   having been sold to Stokely-Van Camp.^[127][14] After the settlement,
   Cade continued to work for the university, and the college of medicine
   named him professor [128]emeritus of nephrology upon his retirement in
   2004. In April 2007, several months before his death, the
   [129]University Athletic Association inducted Cade into the University
   of Florida Athletic Hall of Fame as an "honorary [130]letter
   winner."^[131][14]^[132][19]

   Gatorade, now owned by [133]PepsiCo, is today sold in some eighty
   countries and over fifty various flavors.^[134][7] In stark contrast to
   the forty-three dollars that Cade and his team spent to make the first
   experimental batch of Gatorade in 1965,^[135][7] Gatorade prompted the
   evolution of a multi-billion dollar sports drink industry in the years
   that followed. While he was surprised by its commercial success as a
   sports drink,^[136][4] Cade took greater pride in Gatorade's use in
   hospitals, in post-operative recovery and to treat [137]diarrhea in
   children.^[138][20] Cade's other research included [139]hypertension,
   [140]exercise physiology, [141]autism, [142]schizophrenia and
   [143]kidney disease.^[144][7]

   Cade was an active, life-long member of the [145]Lutheran church, and
   he was recognized by the church with its Wittenberg Award in
   1991.^[146][5] He gave generously to many Lutheran colleges and
   organizations.^[147][5] In their later years, Cade and his wife
   established the [148]Gloria Dei Foundation, an organization that makes
   grants to aid the "poor and underserved."^[149][5]

   Cade was a talented violinist who sometimes played with local
   [150]symphony orchestras.^[151][7] In his later years, Cade acquired
   collections of more than thirty [152]violins and more than sixty
   vintage [153]Studebaker automobiles.^[154][5] He and his wife continued
   to live in the same Gainesville house that they owned before the
   financial success of Gatorade.^[155][14] On November 27, 2007, Cade
   died of [156]kidney failure, at the age of 80, in Gainesville.^[157][4]
   He was survived by his wife Mary, their six children, twenty
   grandchildren and eight great-grandchildren.^[158][2]

   The Cade Museum Foundation, established in 2004 and chaired by Cade's
   daughter, Phoebe Cade Miles,^[159][21] is raising funds to build The
   Cade Museum for Innovation and Invention in Gainesville, with a
   groundbreaking planned for 2013.
