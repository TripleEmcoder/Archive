   Ernest R. Emerson (born March 7, 1955) is an [13]American custom
   [14]knifemaker, [15]martial artist, and edged-weapons expert.^[16][1]
   Originally an engineer and machinist in the [17]aerospace
   industry,^[18][1]^[19][2] Emerson became a knifemaker known for making
   decorative knives but later became better known for his combat knives,
   eventually founding a production company to mass-produce his designs,
   and popularizing a style of knife known as the Tactical-folder.^[20][3]
   Emerson's knives have been displayed as museum pieces, designed for use
   by [21]Navy SEALs,^[22][4]^[23][5] and used by [24]NASA in [25]outer
   space.^[26][6] They have also been featured in films and novels,
   furthering their popularity with collectors.^[27][7]

   Emerson is an accomplished martial artist who has developed a
   [28]combatives system, Emerson Combat Systems, which has been taught to
   [29]police officers, [30]elite military units, and
   civilians.^[31][8]^[32][9]^[33][10]

[[46]edit] Background

   Ernest Emerson was born on March 7, 1955 in a 400-square-foot (37 m^2)
   [47]log cabin built by his grandfather in northern [48]Wisconsin, and
   was raised in a family of farmers, craftsmen, and
   teachers.^[49][8]^[50][11] While attending high school he displayed
   great ability as an athlete, specifically as a [51]wrestler and
   [52]baseball player, being drafted by the [53]St. Louis Cardinals to
   play professional baseball at the age of 17 in the [54]Midwest
   League.^[55][8]

   Emerson began his training in martial arts at the age of 16 with the
   Korean version of [56]Judo known as Yudo, traveling from Wisconsin to
   [57]Minnesota twice a week to attend school.^[58][12] He continued his
   study of the martial arts while attending the [59]University of
   Wisconsin–Madison where he earned a brown belt in [60]Kyokushinkai
   [61]Karate and a [62]black belt in [63]Shotokan Karate while competing
   on the university's karate team.^[64][8] After graduating with degrees
   in [65]physical education and world history, Emerson moved to
   [66]Southern California for the sole purpose of continuing his martial
   arts training at the Filipino Kali Academy.^[67][8]^[68][13]^[69]a
   There he studied Jun Fan Gung Fu, [70]Jeet Kune Do, and [71]Eskrima
   under the tutelage of [72]Dan Inosanto and [73]Richard Bustillo (both
   [74]protégés of the late [75]Bruce
   Lee).^[76][8]^[77][13]^[78][14]^[79][15]^[80]a According to Emerson, he
   could barely afford the [81]US$12.50 monthly dues, and performed
   maintenance and janitorial duties in exchange for
   instruction.^[82][13]^[83]b Emerson subsequently trained in [84]Gracie
   Jiu Jitsu for three years at the original Gracie Academy in
   [85]Torrance, California, under the founders of the Gracie Jiu Jitsu
   system, Rorion and [86]Royce Gracie.^[87][14] Eventually, Emerson
   became an instructor in his own right and combined the principles of
   all these systems.^[88][16] It was also in Southern California where he
   met his wife, Mary, who at the time was one of the world's top female
   practitioners of [89]Jujutsu.^[90][15] During this time, Emerson worked
   as a technician, a machine operator, and eventually a design engineer
   for [91]Hughes Aircraft in [92]El Segundo.^[93][2]^[94][8]^[95][15]

[[96]edit] Early knifemaking

   Emerson's first handmade balisong knife

   Although Emerson credits his grandfather's gift of a [97]Barlow knife
   to him at the age of eight years with starting his interest in knives,
   it was not until later in life that he turned to making them.^[98][11]
   The summer of 1978 found Emerson in need of a [99]balisong knife for
   his study of a [100]Filipino martial art and, unable to afford one on
   his salary,^[101][12] Emerson decided he would attempt to make his own
   instead.^[102][8] He [103]milled and drilled the handles from
   [104]aluminum stock; the knife's blade was a simple steel blank that he
   hand cut with a [105]hacksaw, shaped with [106]files, and heat treated
   at his dining room table with a [107]butane torch.^[108][8]

   When he started classes with this "homemade" knife, his instructors and
   fellow students were impressed with his handiwork and asked him to make
   knives for them.^[109][15] ^[110]cEmerson did so and sold these early
   [111]butterfly knives for just the cost of materials, but he soon
   raised the price to $50 each, as demand for his knives
   increased.^[112][8] Emerson went on to make fixed-blade knives on a
   part-time basis, but upon seeing a Michael Walker handmade folding
   knife at a gun show, he was so impressed by the quality and design that
   he decided he was going to make folding [113]pocketknives from that
   point on.^[114][12]^[115]d Emerson contacted Walker and obtained his
   permission to use the [116]Walker Linerlock mechanism on his own
   knives.^[117][12]^[118][17]^[119][18]

[[120]edit] Pre-tactical models

   Pre-Tac Folders

   Emerson's early folding knives were of the [121]linerlock variety,
   ground by the [122]stock removal method, and utilized rare materials
   from the [123]aerospace industry, including [124]titanium, [125]carbon
   fiber, [126]micarta, and [127]meteorite.^[128][19]^[129]e He also
   incorporated exotic materials common to knifemakers of the time,
   including [130]mother-of-pearl, [131]abalone shell, [132]paua shell,
   staghorn, and rare hardwoods.^[133][20] Writer Paul Basch reported in
   1990 that Emerson refused to use the parts of any animal or plant which
   was an [134]endangered species, noting Emerson as being an
   environmentally conscious knifemaker.^[135][18] The steel used in these
   blades was typically [136]graded ATS-34, AEB-L, or 440C, and the blades
   were either highly polished or hand-rubbed. Emerson also made knives
   with [137]Damascus steel blades.^[138][18] Among custom knife
   collectors and purveyors today, these knives are known as the "Pre-Tac"
   (Pre-Tactical) models.^[139][14] They can be identified by their bright
   [140]anodized titanium bolsters and liners and the predominant use of
   clip point blades.^[141][18] The knives were also noted for their close
   tolerances and precise locking mechanisms.^[142][14] Emerson credits
   the following knifemakers for helping him along during his early years
   as a knifemaker: Michael Walker, Clint Breshears, Bob Engnath, and Jim
   Ferguson.^[143][18]
   The rare Bowie Logo, which appeared on Emerson's early custom knives

   Emerson's first logo or stamp on these knives was "Emerson Knives"
   surrounding the outline of a [144]Bowie knife; accordingly this is
   referred to as the rare "Bowie Logo", appearing on only a very small
   number of knives. When a knife collector asked Emerson if he made Bowie
   knives, Emerson informed him that he did not. The collector then
   advised him to change his logo or he might confuse the knife-buying
   public, as they would ask for Bowie knives instead of linerlock
   folders. Emerson agreed with this assessment, dropping the knife
   outline and the word "knives" from his stamp, using just his name in a
   half-circle on the blade as a logo. This marking is known among
   collectors as the "Half-Moon Logo" and would appear again as a
   transitional mark between the Viper and Specwar lines of
   knives.^[145][14]

   These early knives sold for between $800 and $2,000 each; that,
   combined with his appearances at knife shows and write-ups in knife
   magazines, helped Emerson gain status and credibility as a custom
   knifemaker.^[146][15] Once established as a serious and reputable
   maker, Emerson was soon able to concentrate on making the knives he
   wanted to make—knives designed for use as opposed to show, specifically
   folding-knives designed for combat.^[147][15]

[[148]edit] Viper Knives

   Viper Knives: MV-5, MV-3, and MV-1

   As a direct result of watching his fellow martial artists train with a
   fixed-blade fighting knife in class, yet carry some type of a folding
   knife when they left the training area, Emerson decided there was a
   need for a sturdy folding knife designed primarily for
   combat.^[149][15] Although Emerson had always maintained that the
   knives he made were built as fighting knives first and
   foremost,^[150][18] in October 1988 he stripped down five of these
   designs to simpler materials.^[151][8] He continued his use of linen or
   canvas micarta because of its high tensile strength and superior
   gripping surface when wet; he decided on black or dark grey for the
   color instead of the brighter colors used previously.^[152][14] He
   retained the titanium for the liners and bolsters but chose to
   bead-blast them a flat grey matte color as opposed to the colorful
   anodizing which used to appear on his knives.^[153][14] The reasons for
   using titanium were its exceptional strength-to-weight ratio and
   corrosion resistance.^[154][21]

   Emerson ground the blades of these knives from ATS-34 steel with a
   [155]Rockwell hardness of 57–59 and made them thicker toward the tip,
   creating a stronger-tipped blade than he had made previously.^[156][15]
   He bead-blasted the blades as he did the liners and bolsters instead of
   rubbing or polishing them, giving them a matte finish as opposed to the
   mirror-polished finish common on his earlier knives.^[157][8]^[158][22]
   Emerson designed each one of these knives for a specific purpose. The
   slender Viper 1 and smaller Viper 2 were designed for use as a police
   officer's backup weapon.^[159][14] The Viper 5 was designed to be used
   by naval boat crews, as the handle was contoured with a rear brake so
   as to not slip from the user's hand when wet.^[160][14]
   Viper Knives logo

   Emerson's goal for this Viper line was to produce a working knife which
   would be more resistant to the elements and more durable than the "art
   knives" for which he had become known.^[161][14]^[162][15] These knives
   were created to satisfy the demand for a practical field-grade combat
   knife which could be carried discreetly and accessed quickly.^[163][23]
   He sold these five models under the name "Viper Knives" and changed the
   logo on the blades to read the same.^[164][23]^[165]f Emerson makes
   these models to this day on a custom basis under the names MV1–5, "MV"
   standing for "Model Viper".^[166][8]^[167][22]

[[168]edit] Specwar Knives

   Five variants of the CQC6; top knife is a Super Sized version and with
   the knife below features the Wave.

   In the mid-1980s individual [169]Navy SEALs from a [170]West Coast team
   had been using personally purchased custom fixed-blade knives made by
   Southern California knifemaker [171]Phill Hartsfield.^[172][24]
   Hartsfield's knives are hard ground from differentially heat-treated A2
   tool steel and are known for their distinctive [173]chisel-ground
   blades.^[174][15]^[175][24] More accurately, they are also zero ground;
   that is, the edge has no secondary bevel, minimizing drag when used for
   cutting purposes. Emerson had long been impressed by the cutting
   ability of the chisel-ground edge and had asked Hartsfield's permission
   to incorporate it into his own folding knives, which Hartsfield
   granted.^[176][24] When the SEALs asked Hartsfield to make them a
   folding knife, he informed them that he did not make folding knives and
   referred them to Emerson.^[177][24]^[178][25]^[179][26]

   According to the SEALs' requirements, the knife had to be corrosion
   resistant, designed for easy cleaning in the field, durable enough to
   be used on a daily basis as a tool, and capable as a weapon should the
   need arise.^[180][23] Emerson's folding chisel-ground "tantō" became
   the sixth model in his Viper series and, while a handful of prototypes
   were referred to as "Viper 6", the model was soon named the
   "[181]CQC-6" ([182]CQC refers to close-quarters combat) and was chosen
   by the SEALs for use.^[183][17]^[184][23]^[185][24]^[186][25]^[187]g
   Ownership of a CQC6 soon became something of a status symbol among
   members of various elite military units, including Navy SEALs,
   [188]Army Special Forces, German [189]GSG 9, and [190]British
   SAS.^[191][2]^[192][9]^[193][10]^[194][14]^[195][23]^[196][27]^[197][28
   ]^[198]h^[199]i^[200]j Because of this connection to the Special
   Warfare community, Emerson changed the name of his custom knife line to
   "Specwar Knives", and in 1996 this new designation began appearing in
   the logo on his blades.
   Emerson's Specwar logo

   Other models followed in the Specwar line bearing the CQC Series
   moniker, including the CQC7 which is another chisel-ground tantō-bladed
   folder similar to the CQC6 but with a saber-type handle
   shape.^[201][17]^[202][29]^[203][30] The CQC8 ("Banana Knife") was a
   folder inspired by Bob Taylor's Warrior Knife and is used by British
   SAS troops.^[204][28]^[205][31]^[206][32] The knife also has the
   distinction of being the first folding knife that was designed to be
   [207]ergonomically correct in both forward and reverse grip.^[208][23]
   Its users refer to it as "the finest fighting knife ever
   developed".^[209][32] The CQC9 ("Eagle Knife") is a reverse-curved
   hawkbill blade developed as a backup weapon for an American law
   enforcement agency; it also has an opening hole in the blade licensed
   from knife manufacturer [210]Spyderco.^[211][23]^[212][24]^[213][33]
   Although Emerson has standard models for these custom knives which
   progress in order to CQC16, each one is made individually by
   hand.^[214][8]^[215][15]^[216][24]

[[217]edit] Popularizing the tactical knife

   While not the first knifemaker to build what is known as a tactical
   folding knife, Emerson was one of several makers who popularized the
   concept of the handmade tactical folder in the
   1990s.^[218][2]^[219][27]^[220][34]^[221][35] Emerson's knives also
   began appearing in the [222]Rogue Warrior series of novels written by
   the founder of the US Navy's [223]SEAL Team Six, [224]Richard Marcinko,
   which helped fuel interest among collectors.^[225][7]

   This surge in interest for Emerson's knives soon became overwhelming.
   Although he had been making knives full time since 1994, Emerson was
   still manufacturing these knives in his home garage workshop three
   years later.^[226][15]^[227][20] As Emerson watched his customers' wait
   time expand from two years to seven, he realized that the demand for
   his handmade blades was far outpacing his ability to produce
   them.^[228][20] The first method to bridge this gap between supply and
   demand would be through factory collaborations with established cutlery
   companies.^[229][15]
   Emerson-Neeley Timberline SPECWAR

   Emerson's first collaboration with a knife manufacturer was with
   Timberline Knives in 1993 for his SPECWAR
   model.^[230][27]^[231][36]^[232]k This model featured a one-side
   chisel-ground tantō blade almost 1/4 inch (6.35 mm) thick. Its handle
   was made from fiberglass-reinforced nylon molded around a near-full
   tang. Vaughn Neeley of Timberline designed the sheath.^[233][37] The
   knife was originally a custom piece designed for Naval Special Warfare
   Group One, and this factory version was soon entered in the trials for
   the [234]Navy SEALs knife in 1995.^[235][36]^[236]l Although it was not
   chosen by the Navy, the Emerson-Neeley SPECWAR knife won [237]Blade
   Magazine's 1995 American Made Knife of the Year Award^[238][14] at the
   magazine's Blade Show in [239]Atlanta, Georgia, that same year and was
   displayed as an exhibit at the Metropolitan [240]Museum of Modern Art
   in [241]New York City from May 24 to August 15, 1995.^[242][38]
   Emerson's custom CQC6 alongside Benchmade's 970 (CQC7)

   In 1994 the president of [243]Benchmade Knives, Les DeAsis, approached
   Emerson to manufacture the CQC6 on a larger scale as a factory
   production model.^[244][14] Preferring to keep the CQC6 as a
   custom-only knife, Emerson instead licensed a similar design of his,
   the [245]CQC-7.^[246][39]^[247][40]

   The Benchmade factory version was sold under the model name BM970 or
   BM975 depending on blade length. Other designations followed which
   denoted blade finish,^[248][41] manual or automatic opening, or
   partially serrated blade.^[249][14] The knife retained the profile of
   Emerson's custom piece in addition to the ATS-34 steel and the titanium
   liners.^[250][42] However, on Benchmade's offering the titanium
   bolsters and [251]micarta scales were replaced with G10 [252]fiberglass
   scales, the slotted screws were replaced by [253]Torx head [254]screws,
   and the pocket clip was repositioned so that the knife could be carried
   in the pocket in a tip-down position.^[255][2]^[256][42]

   Despite these changes the knife was true to Emerson's original design,
   and even though it did not have the craftsmanship of a handmade piece
   of cutlery it satisfied customers with their own version of Emerson's
   work at a lower price point and without the five-year
   wait.^[257][17]^[258][24]^[259][40]

   In February 1996, Emerson and his wife, Mary, founded Emerson Knives,
   Inc. (also referred to as EKI) in Torrance, California to manufacture
   knives on a larger scale than he was then capable
   of.^[274][12]^[275][15]^[276][20] This new company would be a distinct
   entity from his Specwar lineup of custom knives, although several
   custom designs would make their way into production from time to time.
   Four years after starting this venture, Emerson sold an entire year's
   worth of production in four hours at the [277]SHOT (Shooting Hunting
   and Outdoor Trade) Show in January 2000.^[278][43]

   Emerson continued to collaborate with other companies on knife-related
   projects as his own company grew. In some cases these collaborators had
   become his competition, such as [279]Gerber Knives. In 2002 Emerson
   collaborated with Gerber Knives to create both companies' first
   [280]automatic opening knife, the Gerber-Emerson
   Alliance.^[281][44]^[282]m In that same year, Emerson collaborated with
   [283]SureFire Flashlights by making an exclusive CQC8 (Banana Knife)
   numbered and marked with the SureFire logo and sold with an identically
   numbered Emerson-marked Centurion C2 CombatLight.^[284][31] In 2005
   Emerson collaborated with Andy Prisco, the CEO of the American Tomahawk
   Company, to produce the CQC-T [285]Tomahawk. This tomahawk features a
   curved head machined from 4140 steel with a rear spike and a
   lightweight fiberglass handle. Although not made by Emerson, the
   tomahawk was designed by him.^[286][45] In 2007, Emerson announced a
   collaboration with custom knifemaker and knife thrower Bobby Branton.
   The collaboration piece is a fixed-blade knife designed primarily for
   [287]knife throwing dubbed the BETT: Branton-Emerson Tactical Thrower.
   Emerson Commander

   One of Emerson's earliest production models, the [288]Commander (winner
   of Blade Magazine's Overall Knife of the Year Award for 1999), is a
   large recurve folding knife based on a special custom design, the
   ES1-M, that he had made for a West Coast Navy SEAL
   Team.^[289][15]^[290][46] The Commander has a hook on the spine of the
   blade (originally designed as a blade catcher) which, when snagged on
   the edge of the pocket or sheath causes the knife blade to open as it
   is drawn.^[291][5]^[292][15]^[293][26] Emerson called this innovation
   the Wave and secured a patent for it in March 1999.^[294][27]^[295][47]
   Emerson's Wave made its way onto most of his knives both in the
   production and custom lines, with the exception of the Viper models. It
   is a required feature on all knives that Emerson supplies to military
   units, search and rescue units, and law enforcement agencies.^[296][48]
   Emerson SARK

   After a disastrous [297]helicopter crash in 1999 resulting in the
   deaths of six [298]Marines and one sailor, the [299]US Navy performed
   an assessment of their equipment and decided among other things that
   they needed a new search and rescue knife.^[300][8]^[301][27] The
   [302]Ka-bar knives issued to the SBUs (Special Boat Units) had
   catastrophically failed to cut the Marines free from their
   webbing.^[303][8]

   The Navy went to Emerson, who designed and fabricated a working
   prototype within 24 hours.^[304][8] They found that it met their needs,
   and the model was dubbed the "[305]SARK" (Search and Rescue
   Knife).^[306][27] The SARK is a folding knife with a
   [307]wharncliffe-style blade and a blunt tip designed so a rescuer
   could cut trapped victims free without stabbing them. The knife
   features Emerson's Wave.^[308][8]^[309]n Seeing another need in the
   [310]police community, Emerson replaced the blunt end of the SARK with
   a pointed end and named it the "P-SARK", or Police Search And Rescue
   Knife. The [311]Ontario, California Police Department consulted Emerson
   to produce written policy for the carry and deployment of the P-SARK
   knives in their department.^[312][49] In 2005, the Navy changed the
   requirements on the SARK to incorporate a guthook on the back of the
   blade for use as a line-cutter.^[313][50] Emerson made the change on
   this model which is only available to the US Navy and the model
   designation is the NSAR (Navy Search And Rescue) Knife.^[314][50]
   Emerson NASA Knife

   In 1999, [315]NASA contracted Emerson to build a knife for use on
   [316]Space Shuttle missions and the [317]International Space
   Station.^[318][51] Rather than design a new model from scratch, NASA
   chose an existing model which already met their specifications, with
   one additional design requirement.^[319][51] The model is a folding
   version of the Specwar knife that Emerson had designed for Timberline
   with the addition of a guthook cut into the [320]tantō point of the
   blade with which [321]astronauts could open their [322]freeze dried
   [323]food packages. The knife is not available for purchase outside of
   NASA.^[324][51]

   On July 1, 2000, Emerson announced his semi-retirement from custom
   knifemaking in order to concentrate on this new production company and
   to fill the thousands of outstanding orders for his custom
   work.^[325][52] He still makes custom knives available for sale at
   knife shows, but takes no orders for new custom
   work.^[326][53]^[327][54] Since 1995 the only way to get a new custom
   knife from Emerson himself is through a [328]lottery held at knife
   shows where he is present. Depending on the size of the show, as many
   as several hundred potential buyers write their names on individual
   pieces of paper at his booth, and at a predetermined time a name is
   drawn. The winner gets a chance to buy one of the custom knives brought
   to the show.^[329][53]^[330][54]^[331]o^[332][32]^[333][55]

   In 2007, Emerson branched out in a new direction, announcing he would
   manufacture twelve custom [334]electric guitars per year.^[335][32] His
   first guitar debuted at Blade Magazine's Blade Show in [336]Atlanta in
   June 2007.^[337][56] In 2008, Emerson opened a clothing company called
   "Emerson Brand Apparel" specializing in MMA and casual clothing. In
   2009 at the annual [338]NRA Convention, Emerson announced a
   collaboration with custom pistol manufacturer [339]Les Baer to produce
   a custom [340]M1911 pistol built to Emerson's
   specifications.^[341][57]^[342][58] In late 2009, Emerson announced a
   collaboration with Spike's Tactical to produce an [343]M-4 Carbine
   based upon his specifications for training, chambered in .22 long rifle
   and in 5.56 NATO.^[344][59]

   In September 2010, Emerson announced a collaboration with [345]Pro-Tech
   Knives to produce an automatic opening version of the CQC-7. In
   November 2010, Emerson's Roadhouse Knife won Knives Illustrated's
   American Made Knife of 2010-2011 Award at the Spirit of Steel Show in
   Knoxville, TN. According to Emerson, the knife will be used as a prop
   on the [346]Sons of Anarchy Television Show.

[[347]edit] Emerson's knives in the media

   Emerson's knives have appeared onscreen in films and television shows.
   In [348]Night of the Running Man, [349]Scott Glenn's character carried
   a [350]mother-of-pearl handled, one-of-a-kind Emerson CQC6. Emerson
   Ravens, neck knives, and the Commander knife were also used as props in
   the short-lived UPN television series [351]Soldier of Fortune,
   Inc.^[352][5] A Commander knife was used by the character of Zak in the
   1998 movie The Placebo Effect; Emerson Knives is thanked in the film
   credits for the knife.

   [353]Ridley Scott's 2001 film [354]Black Hawk Down portrayed soldiers
   carrying Emerson folding knives in the hangar scene, and in [355]Tears
   of the Sun the Kandahar model appeared on [356]Bruce Willis'
   character's web gear, and other actors were seen with Emerson Police
   Utility Knives. An Emerson [357]Karambit is often used as a weapon on
   the television show [358]Burn Notice. [359]Frank Castle used an Emerson
   Karambit to kill an opponent in one of the final scenes in [360]The
   Punisher. [361]Transporter 2 briefly showed an Emerson fixed-blade
   Kandahar knife in the trunk of [362]Jason Statham's car along with
   other weapons as props. An Emerson La Griffe was featured in the 2007
   film: [363]Doomsday.

   Emerson's knives are mentioned in mystery, spy, military, action, and
   adventure novels. At least seven of [364]Richard Marcinko's Rogue
   Warrior novels ([365]Red Cell, Green Team, Task Force Blue, Detachment
   Bravo, SEAL Force Alpha, Violence of Action and Holy Terror)
   prominently make mention of Emerson's knives (CQC6 or CQC7) as a
   regularly carried piece of
   equipment.^[366][7]^[367][60]^[368][61]^[369]p^[370][62] The
   protagonist, Marcinko, uses an Emerson CQC6 or CQC7 on various
   occasions. On page 175 of Task Force Blue, Marcinko remarks that his
   CQC6 was a "personal gift from Ernie Emerson, himself".^[371]q
   Cover art of The Protector

   [372]New York Times bestselling author [373]David Morrell's novel The
   Protector not only has the main character, a former [374]Delta Force
   operator named Cavanaugh, using an Emerson CQC7 knife, but the cover
   art itself is a photographic illustration of a blood-stained Emerson

[[394]edit] Emerson Combat Systems

   CAPTION: Emerson Combat Systems

                                [395]Ecs.jpg
   Emerson Combat Systems
         Focus       [396]Hybrid
   Country of origin [397]United States [398]U.S.
        Creator      Ernest R Emerson
      Parenthood     [399]Jeet Kune Do, [400]Boxing, [401]Brazilian Jujitsu

   Emerson's study and instruction of martial arts is ongoing.^[402][12]
   He has accumulated 35 years of experience in a variety of styles and
   philosophies of combat.^[403][8] Drawing upon this base of knowledge
   and experience he has developed a combatives system known as Emerson
   Combat Systems, which has been taught to hundreds of law enforcement
   agencies, members of the U.S. military, and
   civilians.^[404][8]^[405][12]^[406][15]^[407][24]^[408][27]^[409][73]

   Emerson Combat Systems is designed to give the student the tools to
   endure the first few seconds of a violent attack.^[410][74] The system
   is based upon the recognition of two factors: the human body's
   [411]physiological response to [412]stress and its reduction to the use
   of gross motor skills.^[413][16] According to Emerson, the techniques
   are based upon the physical and instinctual laws that govern the
   survival instincts and physical function of a human being.^[414][74]
   Ernest Emerson demonstrates an elbow strike.

   This system is characterized by training as realistically as possible
   (not training in workout gear for example) and using flowing, dynamic
   concepts (for example, integrated fighting^[415]t and weapon
   transition^[416]u) with a strong emphasis placed on overall physical
   fitness.^[417][75] Emerson maintains he does not teach a "martial art"
   encumbered by ritual or sporting aspects but a combatives system where
   the goal is more than simple self defense.^[418][74]^[419][76]^[420]v

   Emerson has been consulted as a technical advisor to television and
   movie productions including [421]National Geographic, specifically a
   program titled [422]Fight Science, due in part to his position as the
   Hand To Hand Combat Instructor for [423]Harry Humphries' Global Studies
   Group Incorporated, a company that teaches police and military tactics
   to law enforcement agencies and film production
   crews.^[424][10]^[425][77]^[426]w

   Emerson has written over 30 articles on hand-to-hand combat, knife
   fighting, history, and knifemaking for publications including
   [427]Blade Magazine,^[428][78] American Cop Magazine,^[429][79] Martial
   Arts Experts,^[430][80] Journal of Modern Combatives,^[431][74] Inside
   Kung-Fu,^[432][76] Black Belt Magazine,^[433][81] Police
   Magazine,^[434][82] and American Handgunner Magazine.^[435][83]

       blade attack. Half way through the second eight hours, I zigged
