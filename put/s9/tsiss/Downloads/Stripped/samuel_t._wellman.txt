Samuel T. Wellman

   Died July 11, 1919(1919-07-11) (aged 72)

   Samuel Thomas Wellman, (February 5, 1847 – July 11, 1919) was an
   [9]American [10]steel industry pioneer, industrialist, and prolific
   [11]inventor. Wellman was a close friend of electrical pioneer
   [12]George Westinghouse,^[13][1] and [14]Charles M. Schwab of
   [15]Bethlehem Steel described Samuel T. Wellman as "the man who did
   more than any other living person in the development of steel".^[16][2]
   Wellman was also president of the [17]American Society of Mechanical
   Engineers from 1901 to 1902.^[18][3]

[[26]edit] Early life

   Born in [27]Wareham, Massachusetts in 1847, Wellman was the son of a
   [28]Nashua [29]Iron Company superintendent and a
   great-great-great-great-great-grandson of [30]Puritan [31]Thomas
   Wellman.^[32][4] Wellman received his formal engineering training from
   [33]Norwich University in [34]Norwich, Vermont, and served as a
   corporal with the First New Hampshire [35]Heavy Artillery during the
   [36]Civil War. Shortly after the war, Wellman married Julia A. Ballard,
   with whom he had five children.^[37][2]

[[38]edit] Career and influence on the steel industry

   Wellman began his career working at the Nashua Iron Company. He was
   encouraged by his father to build a [39]regenerative gas furnace for
   the company. Wellman did this, impressing [40]Carl Wilhelm Siemens, who
   immediately hired him to establish the first [41]crucible-steel furnace
   in America.^[42][2] Wellman went on to improve upon the [43]open-hearth
   process of steel [44]rail production, which in turn had improved upon
   the [45]Bessemer process.^[46][5] In 1869, Wellman built the first
   commercially successful open-hearth furnace in America at the [47]Bay
   State Iron Works in [48]South Boston.^[49][6]

   Furnaces were not Wellman's only contribution to the steel industry. He
   was also instrumental in the development of the [50]Hulett
   unloader,^[51][7] which allowed the unloading of [52]taconite from the
   [53]iron ore boats of the [54]Great Lakes, particularly on [55]Lake
   Erie. In addition to improvements on the Hulett unloader, other
   important inventions include an open hearth charging machine and a
   [56]hydraulic crane. Following an unsuccessful venture with his
   half-brother, Wellman later founded the [57]Wellman-Seaver-Morgan
   Engineering Company in [58]Cleveland, Ohio,^[59][8] which continues
   under a different name to this day.^[60][2]
