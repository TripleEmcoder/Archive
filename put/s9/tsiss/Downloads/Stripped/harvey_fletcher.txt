   Harvey Fletcher (September 11, 1884 – July 23, 1981) was an
   [26]American [27]physicist. Known as the "father of stereophonic sound"
   he is credited with the invention of the [28]audiometer^[[29]dubious –
   [30]discuss] and [31]hearing aid.^[32][1] He is remembered as a
   trail-blazing investigator into the nature of speech and hearing, and
   for his numerous contributions in acoustics, electrical engineering,
   speech, medicine, music, atomic physics, sound pictures, and education.

[[38]edit] Early career

   Fletcher was born in [39]Provo, [40]Utah. He graduated from Brigham
   Young High School in 1904. He enrolled at [41]Brigham Young University
   (BYU) and graduated in 1907 with a bachelor's degree. As a graduate
   student at the [42]University of Chicago, his dissertation research was
   on methods to determine the charge of an electron. This included the
   now famous [43]oil drop experiment commonly attributed to his advisor
   and collaborator, [44]Robert Millikan. Professor Millikan took sole
   credit, in return for Fletcher claiming full authorship on a related
   result for his dissertation. Fletcher's contributions were
   detail-oriented but necessary for a successful experiment, in which he
   incorporated, among other things, experience with projection
   lanterns.^[45][2] Millikan went on to win the 1923 [46]Nobel Prize for
   Physics, in part for this work, and Fletcher kept the agreement a
   secret until his death.^[47][3]

[[48]edit] Later years

   Among the work that he is best known for are Fletcher's contributions
   to the theory of [49]speech perception. He showed that speech features
   are usually spread over a wide frequency range, and developed the
   [50]articulation index to approximately quantify the quality of a
   speech channel.^[51][4] He also developed the concepts of,
   [52]equal-loudness contours, loudness scaling and summation, and the
   [53]critical band.^[54][5] As Director of Research at [55]Bell Labs, he
   oversaw research in electrical sound recording, including more than 100
   stereo recordings with conductor [56]Leopold Stokowski in
   1931-2.^[57][6]^[58][7]

   Much of his research is considered to be authoritative, and his books,
   Speech and Hearing and Speech and Hearing in Communication, are
   landmark treatises on the subject.

[[59]edit] Honors

   Dr. Fletcher was elected an honorary fellow of [60]Acoustical Society
   of America in 1949, the second person to receive this honor after
   [61]Thomas Edison. He was president of the American Society for Hard of
   Hearing, an honorary member of the American Otological Society and an
   honorary member of the [62]Audio Engineering Society. In 1924 he was
   awarded the Louis E. Levy Medal for physical measurements of audition
   by the [63]Franklin Institute. He was President of the [64]American
   Physical Society which is the leading Physics society in America. In
   1937 he was elected vice-president of the American Association for the
   Advancement of Science. He was a member of the [65]National Academy of
   Sciences. He was also a member of the National Hearing Division
   Committee of Medical Sciences. He was given the Progress Medal Award by
   the American Academy of Motion Pictures, in Hollywood. For eight years
   he acted as National Councilor for the [66]Ohio State University
   Research Foundation.

   In 2010, Fletcher was honored by BYU as the Founding Dean of the BYU
   College of Engineering ^[67][8] (now the [68]Ira A. Fulton College of
   Engineering and Technology).

   He died on July 23, 1981, after a [69]stroke.
