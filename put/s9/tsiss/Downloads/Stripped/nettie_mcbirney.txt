   Nettie Williams McBirney (November 24, 1887^[18][1] – December 16,
   1982) was a [19]Tulsa inventor, writer and entrepreneur, who wrote a
   cooking column under the pseudonym Aunt Chick for the [20]Tulsa Daily
   World from 1935 to 1955.^[21][2]

[[29]edit] Biography

   Born November 24, 1887, Nettie McBirney earned a [30]home economics
   degree from the [31]Stout Institute in [32]Menomonie,
   Wisconsin.^[33][3] She moved to [34]Claremore, Oklahoma in 1909 to
   teach home economics. Two years later, she became supervisor of home
   economics at [35]Muskogee schools. After marrying [36]Sam P. McBirney,
   coach of the [37]University of Tulsa football team and vice president
   of the [38]National Bank of Commerce, she settled in Tulsa in
   1916.^[39][4]

   In 1935, she approached Tulsa World editor N.G. Henthorne about writing
   a cooking column that contained simple advice and taught [40]cooking
   techniques.^[41][4] "Kitchen Log" ran five times a week in the World
   through 1955. She also became a demonstration chef, first at
   [42]Vandever's Department Store and other stores in Tulsa, and later in
   many other parts of the country.

   McBirney began inventing kitchen aids during the [43]Depression, such
   as a non-stick pastry canvas and [44]rolling pin cover and a pie pan
   that promised perfect bottom [45]crusts on [46]pies.^[47][5] The
   invention she became best known for was her Cooky Molding Cutter, now
   known as Gramma's Cutter, a [48]cookie cutter designed to easily
   release the molded cookie dough and maintain a 3-D appearance. She
   began selling them in 1948 and they quickly caught on; [49]Princess
   Margaret purchased a set of the cookie cutters for [50]Prince Charles
   in 1952, and [51]Wrigley purchased, then sold 70,000 sets as a special
   promotion the same year.^[52][3]

[[53]edit] Marriage and children

   She married banker Samuel P. McBirney in 1913. The couple had four
   children: Susan Bush, Williams, Samuel, and Mary Megan Bryan.^[54][6]

[[55]edit] Death and afterward

   Nettie McBirney died on December 16, 1982. Her collection of about
   1,000 [56]cookbooks was donated to the [57]Tulsa City-County Library in
   1973.^[58][7]

[[59]edit] Published works

   While writing her Kitchen Log column, Aunt Chick also found time to
   write several cookbooks, most of which dealt with pies and other
   [60]desserts. Most notable is Aunt Chick’s Pies, which sold over
   650,000 copies.
