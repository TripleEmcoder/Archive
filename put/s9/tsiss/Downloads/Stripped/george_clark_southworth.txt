   George Clark Southworth (August 24, 1890 - July 6, 1972), who published
   as G. C. Southworth, was a prominent [12]American [13]radio engineer
   best known for his role in the discovery of [14]waveguides in the early
   1930s.

[[15]edit] Biography

   Southworth was born in [16]Little Cooley, Pennsylvania, graduated in
   1914 with a physics degree from [17]Grove City College, and studied one
   year at [18]Columbia University. In June 1917 he joined the
   [19]National Bureau of Standards, then in 1918 moved to [20]Yale
   University to teach in a [21]Signal Corps school. He remained at Yale
   to complete a doctorate in 1923 on the measurement of the
   [22]dielectric constant of water at frequencies above 15 MHz.

   Southworth left Yale for a position with the [23]American Telephone and
   Telegraph Company, where he first helped edit the [24]Bell System
   Technical Journal, but then switched to researching [25]shortwave
   [26]radio propagation.

   In 1931 he began to study wave propagation in dielectric rods, by early
   1932 observed wave propagation in a water-filled copper pipe, and by
   May 1933 transmitted waves through air-filled copper pipes up to 20
   feet in length. (He later recalled that the first message sent through
   a waveguide was "Send money.") After he constructed a 5-in.-diameter
   [27]waveguide with a length of 875 feet, the project was moved to the
   [28]Bell Telephone Laboratories in [29]Holmdel, New Jersey, where he
   spent the rest of his career until retirement in 1955.

   Southworth received the [30]Morris N. Liebmann Award in 1938, and the
   [31]IEEE Medal of Honor in 1963 "For pioneering contributions to
   microwave radio physics, to [32]radio astronomy, and to waveguide
   transmission."

[[33]edit] Selected works

     * Principles and applications of wave-guide transmission, New York,
       Van Nostrand [1950], xi, 689 p. illus. 24 cm. Bell Telephone
       Laboratories series. LCCN 50009834.
     * Forty years of Radio Research, 1962.
