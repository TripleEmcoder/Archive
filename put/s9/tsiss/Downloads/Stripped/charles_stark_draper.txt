   Charles Stark Draper (October 2, 1901 – July 25, 1987) was an American
   [14]scientist and [15]engineer, often referred to as "the father of
   [16]inertial navigation." He was the founder and director of the
   [17]MIT Instrumentation Laboratory, later renamed the [18]Charles Stark
   Draper Laboratory, which under his direction designed and built the
   [19]Apollo Guidance Computer for NASA, which made the Apollo moon
   landings possible.

[[24]edit] Life and work

   Born in [25]Windsor, [26]Missouri, he attended the [27]University of
   Missouri in 1917, then transferred to [28]Stanford University,
   [29]California in 1919, from which he earned a [30]B.A. in
   [31]psychology in 1922. After Stanford, he attended [32]Massachusetts
   Institute of Technology (MIT), from which he earned an [33]S.B. in
   electrochemical engineering in 1926, and an [34]S.M. and [35]Sc.D. in
   [36]physics in 1928 and 1938 respectively.^[37][1]

   He started teaching while at MIT, first as an assistant, then quickly
   became a full professor in aeronautical engineering in 1939. It was
   here that he founded the Instrumentation Laboratory in the 1930s, later
   spun off as [38]The Charles Stark Draper Laboratory, Inc. In 1961,
   Draper and the Instrumentation Lab were awarded the first contract
   given out for the [39]Apollo program to send humans to the moon, which
   had just been announced by President John F. Kennedy. This led to the
   creation of the [40]Apollo Guidance Computer, a one-cubic-foot computer
   that controlled the navigation and guidance of the Lunar Excursion
   Module to the surface of the moon during six successful landings.

   His interest in flight instrumentation also spun from becoming a pilot
   with an engineering training in the 1930s: although he failed to become
   an Air Corps pilot^[41][2], he learned to fly by enrolling in a
   civilian course.

   Draper invented and developed [42]inertial navigation, a technology
   used in [43]aircraft, space vehicles, and [44]submarines that allows
   such vehicles to navigate by sensing changes in direction, using
   [45]gyroscopes, and speed, using accelerometers. A pioneering figure in
   the aircraft engineering field, he also contributed to the Apollo space
   program with his knowledge of guidance systems. For his inventions and
   contributions, Draper was inducted to the [46]National Inventors Hall
   of Fame in 1981.

   He was awarded the [47]Howard N. Potts Medal in 1960. In 1964, he was
   awarded the [48]National Medal of Science.^[49][3]

   The [50]Charles Stark Draper Prize is a prominent prize in engineering
   devoted to the memory of Charles Stark Draper. Charles Stark Draper's
   relatives were rather prominent in his Missouri birthplace, including
   his cousin, Governor [51]Lloyd C. Stark.
