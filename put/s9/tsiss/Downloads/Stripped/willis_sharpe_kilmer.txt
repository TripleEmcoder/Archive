   Willis Sharpe Kilmer (October 18, 1869 - July 12, 1940), son of Jonas
   M. Kilmer and Julia E. Sharpe, was a marketing pioneer,
   [17]newspaperman, and [18]horse breeder. Born in [19]Brooklyn, New
   York, he graduated from [20]Cornell University in [21]1890^[22][1].
   Kilmer was perhaps best known for advertising and promoting his Uncle's
   [23]Swamp Root [24]patent medicine formula until it became a household
   name.

   Dr. S. Andral Kilmer invented the formula and invited his brother Jonas
   to run the business end of the company in [25]1881. Jonas would later
   buy out his brother and bring in his son Willis to handle the marketing
   and advertising. As the business became successful, they eventually
   constructed the six-story Kilmer Building in downtown [26]Binghamton,
   NY as their manufacturing and business headquarters in 1903. The Swamp
   Root formulation fell out of favor with the advent of the [27]Pure Food
   and Drug Act of 1906, which resulted in the federal government imposing
   testing and labeling requirements on a variety of products,
   specifically [28]patent medicines with dubious claims.
   Kilmer mausoleum in 2009

   The decline in the patent medicine business led to a branching out into
   other businesses. He was extensively involved in real estate, owning a
   family mansion in [29]Binghamton, NY, constructing the twelve story
   Press Building in downtown Binghamton as a home for another new
   business he created, [30]The Binghamton Press Co., building several
   other less-prominent buildings in downtown Binghamton, and three racing
   stables and estates: Sun Briar Court in Binghamton, [31]Court Manor in
   the [32]Shenandoah Valley of [33]Virginia, and Remlik, on the banks of
   Virginia's [34]Rappahannock River. Kilmer's private yacht Remlik was
   purchased by the US Navy during World War I and converted into the USS
   Remlik, the name being Kilmer spelled backwards.

   Kilmer died in 1940 from [35]pneumonia having amassed a fortune of some
   $15 million, mostly from the sale of the [36]patent medicine Swamp Root
   tonic, which is still for sale
   today.^[37][2]^[38][3]^[39][4]^[40][5]^[41][6]^[42][7]^[43][8]^[44][9]^
   [45][10]^[46][11] After his death, Kilmer was interred at a mausoleum
   in Floral Park Cemetery in the Binghamton suburb of [47]Johnson City,
   NY.

[[48]edit] Thoroughbred racing

   Kilmer was the breeder of [49]Reigh Count, the winner of the 1928
   [50]Kentucky Derby. He was the owner of [51]Exterminator, the winner of
   the 1918 Kentucky Derby and 1922 [52]American Horse of the Year, and
   the breeder and owner of [53]Sun Beau, the largest money maker until
   [54]Seabiscuit in 1939. Both Exterminator and Sun Beau were elected to
   the [55]National Museum of Racing and Hall of Fame.
