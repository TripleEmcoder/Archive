   Thomas Kimmwood Peters (June 27, 1879 – 1973) was a pioneer
   [21]American motion picture producer, [22]newsreel cameraman,
   photographer, educator, and
   inventor.^[23][1]^[24][2]^[25][3]^[26][4]^[27][5]^[28][6]

[[38]edit] Education

     * Attended public elementary schools and high school in the Los
       Angeles area, 1884-94.^[39][6]
     * Attended Peace College in Mexico City from 1896 to
       1898.^[40][1]^[41][6]
     * Earned [42]Ph.D. at [43]Oglethorpe University,
       1939.^[44][1]^[45][6]

[[46]edit] Association with the motion picture industry

   Peters began his photography work in the motion picture industry around
   1899,^[47][6]^[48][7] with the [49]Pathe Freres film company of
   France.^[50][6]^[51][7] Later, when he left them, he continued to work
   throughout Europe at various companies in the motion picture industry
   to develop his skills.^[52][6]^[53][7] He also did photography and
   motion picture work at [54]Karnak and [55]Luxor in
   Egypt.^[56][3]^[57][6]

   Peters was associated with the Cosmos Film Company in [58]San
   Francisco, California, which later became Exactus Photo Film
   Corporation of [59]Palo Alto.^[60][6]^[61][7] He became its first
   president and general manager when it officially started operations on
   August 28, 1914.^[62][7] The company stated that its purpose was
   “ to produce, sell, rent and exchange educational and industrial moving
     picture films for the use of schools, academies, and universities
     throughout the state of California, and later throughout the United
     States.^[63][7]                                                        ”

   In the beginning, Exactus had a list of well-respected California
   educators to serve on its board of directors.^[64][7] However, a lack
   of good business management and closing skills in obtaining financial
   backing caused its failure only two years later.^[65][7] There were
   also clashes of misunderstandings between the educators and Exactus'
   technicians that exacerbated the situation.^[66][7] By the end of 1916,
   Peters held an auction of the physical items held by the bankrupt
   company to raise money to pay off its creditors.^[67][7] What little
   stock left was turned over to the Palo Alto Film Company.^[68][7]

   Peters kept the technical and educational value of the Exactus films at
   a high quality in spite of these tumultuous times.^[69][7] At the
   [70]Panama-Pacific International Exposition in 1915, his company won
   two gold medals and one bronze medal for films they had
   produced.^[71][7]

[[72]edit] Association with Oglethorpe University

   Oglethorpe University
   Main article: [73]Crypt of Civilization

   [74]Thornwell Jacobs, originator of the [75]Crypt of Civilization (the
   world's first modern [76]time capsule located at [77]Oglethorpe
   University), hired Peters to serve as the project [78]archivist and
   general manager of development and
   construction.^[79][1]^[80][2]^[81][5]^[82][6]

   One of the reasons Peters was hired was for his unique skills in
   photography.^[83][2] He used his knowledge to preserve on [84]microfilm
   and film footage items to be entered in the Crypt for a 6,000-year
   storage.^[85][2]^[86][6] Peters recorded on film such historical
   figures as [87]Adolf Hitler and [88]Joseph Stalin. He also included
   President [89]Franklin D. Roosevelt and [90]Benito Mussolini.^[91][2]

   Peters and his staff of student assistants worked on the Crypt project
   from 1937 until 1940 when it was sealed permanently.^[92][2]^[93][6]
   They put on cellulose acetate microfilm many historical events of the
   19th and 20th centuries.^[94][2] The microfilm reels were placed into
   airtight receptacles for preservation.^[95][2] The staff and Peters
   initiated backup methods in case the acetate didn't survive.^[96][2]
   One method Peters developed was duplicate "metal film."^[97][2] It was
   as thin as paper.^[98][2]

   Peters also worked on developing Oglethorpe University's historical
   photographic collections.^[99][4]^[100][6] Peters was also director of
   the China Air Force School at Oglethorpe University,
   1945-46.^[101][1]^[102][6] He was also professor of audio-visual
   education at the university.^[103][1]

   According to Oglethorpe University Peters had been the only newsreel
   photographer to film the San Francisco earthquake of 1906 and the
   associated fire. He was also the first to film the construction of the
   Panama Canal.^[104][3]^[105][4]^[106][5]^[107][6]

[[108]edit] Outline of career

     * Artist with International Botanical Expedition to Yucatán,
       1898.^[109][1]^[110][6]
     * Camerman with Pathe Freres, Vincennes, France,
       1899.^[111][1]^[112][6]
     * Toured Europe and Northe Africa making motion pictures with a
       [113]Kinetoscope, 1900.^[114][1]^[115][6]
     * Freelanced in Paris, 1902.^[116][1]^[117][6]
     * Opened Peters Scenic Studio in Los Angeles, 1902.^[118][6]
     * President of Peters Photographic Expeditions. Made 5 expeditions to
       Asian countries, Australia and the South Seas,
       1904-05.^[119][1]^[120][6]
     * Motion pictures of San Francisco earthquake aftermath,
       1906.^[121][3]^[122][4]^[123][6]
     * Motion pictures of construction of the Panama Canal,
       1909.^[124][1]^[125][3]^[126][4]^[127][6]
     * Technical director of several motion picture companies.^[128][6]
     * Author and correlator of standard text films for classroom use at
       Palo Alto and F.B.O. Studio, Hollywood.^[129][6]
     * Inaugurated first regulator teaching films in New York Schools,
       1920.^[130][1]^[131][6]
     * Professor of psychology training specialist and manufacturing
       research engineer at Lockheed Aircraft Company.^[132][6]
     * Historian at the Los Angeles County Hollywood Museum Motion Picture
       TV.^[133][6]
     * Owner of Photographic Museum, Frontier Village, San Jose,
       California.^[134][6]

     * Portals of the Past ^[136][6]
     * Sunoef Dixie (the script) ^[137][6]
     * Trap to Catch a Shadow ^[138][6]
     * History of Motion Picture Mechanisms (autobiography) ^[139][6]
     * Technique of Concertography: Prelude to the Atomic Era ^[140][6]

[[141]edit] Inventions

     * Invented several models of the Exactus camera, 1912 -
       1914.^[142][6]^[143][7]
     * Invented the first microfilm camera using 35 mm film to photograph
       documents, 1915.^[144][1]^[145][2]^[146][3]^[147][6]^[148][8]
     * Invented and patented many vacuum and rare gas devices, including
       first commercial rare gas tubes, 1924.^[149][1]^[150][6]
