   Dr. Ann A. Kiessling (born March 29, 1942) is an [12]American
   reproductive biologist and is currently one of the leaders in human
   parthenogenic stem cell research at The [13]Bedford Stem Cell Research
   Foundation.^[14][1] She also has an appointment in the Department of
   Surgery at [15]Harvard Medical School^[16][2]

   Dr. Kiessling is noted for her discovery of [17]reverse transcriptase
   activity in normal human cells (Kiessling & Goulian).^[18][3] This
   report pioneered the importance of naturally occurring retrovirus
   sequences in human genes, now thought to be important to the genetic
   plasticity involved in human evolution and biology. Prior to this
   discovery, it had been assumed that reverse transcriptase was an enzyme
   found only in [19]retroviruses(such as [20]HIV). To understand the
   normal biologic role of reverse transcriptase, Dr. Kiessling began to
   study eggs and early cleaving embryos.^[[21]citation needed] Her dual
   interests in [22]virology and [23]reproductive biology led to research
   in semen transmission of [24]Human Immunodeficiency Virus ([25]HIV),
   and the creation of the first laboratory for [26]Human In Vitro
   Fertilization ([27]IVF) in [28]Oregon in the early 1980s. [29]Harvard
   Medical School recruited Dr. Kiessling in 1985, where she continues her
   research today.^[[30]citation needed]

   The need to conduct biomedical research in areas not funded by the
   federal government led to the incorporation of the Bedford Stem Cell
   Research Foundation.^[31][4] The Foundation's controversial [32]Special
   Program of Assisted Reproduction (SPAR) has helped more than 70 couples
   [33][1] affected by HIV disease have healthy babies.^[34][5] Because of
   this success, more than two-dozen fertility centers throughout the
   country have implemented the SPAR program, allowing couples to seek
   care close to home.^[[35]citation needed]

   The techniques developed for the [36]Special Program of Assisted
   Reproduction have now been extended to other diseases of the male
   genitourinary tract, such as prostatitis and bladder infections.
   Expertise in human egg biology led Dr. Kiessling to develop the
   country's first human egg donor program for [37]stem cell research in
   2000. It remains a research focus today.^[[38]citation needed]

   Dr. Kiessling has published more than 100 scientific papers and given
   more than 60 lectures to audiences around the world. Her writings can
   be found in publications such as Nature, Lancet and in proceedings of
   the [39]National Academy of Science. Among her noted publications is
   the first comprehensive look at the influence of accurate science
   terminology on laws titled, "What is an Embryo," published by the
   Connecticut Law Review^[40][6] along with rejoinders by Dr. [41]Harold
   Shapiro, Prof [42]John A. Robertson, Prof. Lars Noah, and Father Kevin
   P. Quinn. The law review specifically addresses the controversy of all
   of the entities that are currently called embryos with regards to
   embryonic stem cell research legislation around the wold.^[[43]citation
   needed]

   Dr. Kiessling has been the focus of articles in [44]The Boston Globe,
   [45]Wall Street Journal, [46]Los Angeles Times, [47]Associated Press
   and [48]Newsweek. In 2003, Dr. Kiessling wrote Human Embryonic Stem
   Cells: An Introduction to the Science and Therapeutic Potential, the
   first textbook on the controversial topic.^[49][7]

   Dr. Kiessling is a current member of the California ([50]California
   Constitution Article XXXV) and Connecticut Stem Cell Research Advisory
   Boards,^[51][8] and a member of the Embryonic Stem Cell Research
   Oversight Committees (ESCROS) for Harvard University,^[52][9]
   [53]Joslin Diabetes Center and Children's Hospital.^[54][10]

[[64]edit] Background

   Dr. Kiessling was born in [65]Baker City, Oregon as Ann Anderson. Her
   father, Col. William C. Anderson, was a decorated squadron commander in
   the [66]US Air Force during [67]World War II. She graduated from
   Klamath Falls High School in 1960 and attended [68]University of
   Virginia where she received the first of her two bachelor's degrees in
   [69]Nursing. In 1966 she received her second bachelor's degree in
   [70]Chemistry from [71]Central Washington University where she also
   received her master's degree in [72]Organic Chemistry in 1967. In 1971
   she earned her PhD from [73]Oregon State University in [74]Biochemistry
   and [75]Biophysics. She did postdoctoral research at [76]Fred
   Hutchinson Cancer Research Center, [77]Memorial Sloan-Kettering Cancer
   Center, and [78]University of California, [79]San Diego.

[[80]edit] Family

   Dr. Kiessling is the mother of three daughters and a son.
