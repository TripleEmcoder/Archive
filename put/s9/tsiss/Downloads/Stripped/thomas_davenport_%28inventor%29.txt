   Thomas Davenport (9 July 1802 – 6 July 1851) was a [12]Vermont
   [13]blacksmith who invented the first American [14]DC electrical motor
   in 1834.^[15][1]

   He lived in Forest Dale, a village near the town of [16]Brandon.

   As early as 1834, he developed a battery-powered electric motor. He
   used it to operate a small-model car on a short section of track,
   paving the way for the later electrification of [17]streetcars.^[18][2]

   Davenport's 1833 visit to the Penfield and Taft iron works at [19]Crown
   Point, New York, where an [20]electromagnet was operating, based on the
   design of [21]Joseph Henry, was an impetus for his electromagnetic
   undertakings. Davenport bought an electromagnet from the Crown Point
   factory and took it apart to see how it worked. Then he forged a better
   iron core and redid the wiring, using silk from his wife's wedding
   gown^[22][3].

   With his wife [23]Emily, and a colleague Orange Smalley, Davenport
   received the first American [24]patent on an electric machine in 1837,
   U. S. Patent No. 132.

   In 1849, [25]Charles Grafton Page, the Washington scientist and
   inventor, commenced a project to build an electromagnetically powered
   locomotive, with substantial funds appropriated by the US Senate.
   Davenport challenged the expenditure of public funds, arguing for the
   motors he had already invented. In 1851, Page's full sized
   electromagnetically operated locomotive was put to a calamity-laden
   test on the rail line between Washington and Baltimore^[26][4].
