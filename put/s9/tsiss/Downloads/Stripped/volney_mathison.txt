Volney Mathison

   Volney G. Mathison (also known by the [9]pseudonym Dex Volney) was an
   [10]American [11]chiropractor, writer, and inventor of the
   [12]E-meter.^[13][1]^[14][2]

[[26]edit] Family

   In 1935, Mathison was married to Jean Darrell, a music librarian for
   [27]NBC.^[28][3] She died in November 1964.^[29][4]

[[30]edit] Career

[[31]edit] Writer

   Dust jacket of The Radiobuster by Volney G. Mathison

   In 1921, Mathison wrote the fictional short story "A Phony Phone",
   which was published in [32]Radio News edited by [33]Hugo
   Gernsback.^[34][5] In 1924, he wrote the fictional book The
   Radiobuster: Being Some of the Adventures of Samuel Jones, Deep Sea
   Wireless Operator.^[35][6] The book is listed in American Fiction,
   1901-1925: A Bibliography.^[36][6] Mathison's story "The Death Bottle"
   was published in Weird Tales in March 1925.^[37][7] He also wrote
   stories which were published under the [38]pseudonym of "Dex
   Volney".^[39][7] His pieces as "Dex Volney" were of the [40]Western
   genre, and set in [41]Alaska.^[42][7] According to Science-Fiction: The
   Gernsback Years, Mathison was "a prolific author" under this
   pseudonym.^[43][7] As Dex Volney, he wrote popular stories published by
   [44]Street & Smith.^[45][8]

   In the June 1929 issue of [46]Amazing Stories, Mathison's story "The
   Mongolian's Ray" appeared and was promoted on the cover.^[47][9]
   Forrest J. Ackerman and Brad Linaweaver write in the book Worlds of
   Tomorrow, "In this story, he created the fictional device that shortly
   after the introduction of Dianetics, morphed into reality as the
   E-meter employed today to supposedly reveal the personalities of
   individuals interested in becoming 'clears' in the Dianetic
   regimen."^[48][9] Mathison's story "Thor Olsen's Ace" was selected for
   inclusion in The World's Best Short Stories of 1930.^[49][10]

[[50]edit] Inventor

   In 1935, Mathison was employed building [51]short wave radios.^[52][3]
   Mathison invented a device called an electroencephaloneuromentimograph
   or [53]E-meter.^[54][11] He came up with the design for the device and
   subsequently built it in the 1940s.^[55][1]^[56][12] It was initially
   known as the "Mathison Electropsychometer".^[57][13] The E-meter "has a
   needle that swings back and forth across a scale when a patient holds
   on to two electrical contacts".^[58][1] Mathison was a [59]chiropractor
   and [60]psychoanalyst.^[61][1]^[62][11]^[63][14]^[64][15] He used the
   device with his patients in order to investigate their inner
   problems.^[65][13] He then employed self-hypnosis tapes, and instructed
   his patients to use these in order to address their "inner"
   issues.^[66][13] The device became popular and was used among other
   chiropractors.^[67][1] John Freeman writes in Suppressed and Incredible
   Inventions, "Recalling my visits at the height of his career, I
   remember that, while his results were outstanding, he was typically
   fought by the Medical Profession."^[68][13]
   Volney Mathison uses an E-meter to analyze a patient, circa 1951

   Mathison was a follower of [69]Dianetics founded by [70]L. Ron
   Hubbard.^[71][12] Hubbard incorporated Mathison's device into
   [72]Scientology practices.^[73][11] Hubbard often called the inventor
   of the E-meter simply "Mathison" in his writings.^[74][11] According to
   author [75]Paulette Cooper, Scientologists erroneously referred to the
   inventor of the E-meter as "Olin Mathison".^[76][11] Simon Singh and
   Edzard Ernst write in Trick or Treatment that "The E-meter was also
   widely used by the Church of Scientology, so much so that many
   Scientologists believe that it was invented by their founder L. Ron
   Hubbard."^[77][1]

   After establishing usage of the E-meter in Dianetics, Hubbard sought
   for Mathison to turn over patent rights of the device to
   him.^[78][14]^[79][15] Mathison refused to give up the patent rights
   over the device, wishing that it remain the "Mathison
   E-meter".^[80][14]^[81][15]^[82][16] Usage of the E-meter in Dianetics
   practices was subsequently stopped by Hubbard in
   1954.^[83][15]^[84][16] In writing that use of the E-meter should be
   discontinued, Hubbard said, "Yesterday, we used an instrument called an
   E-Meter to register whether or not the process was still getting
   results so that the auditor would know how long to continue it. While
   the E-Meter is an interesting investigation instrument and has played
   its part in research, it is not today used by the auditor.... As we
   long ago suspected, the intervention of a mechnical [[85]sic] gadget
   between the auditor and the preclear had a tendency to depersonalize
   the session...."^[86][16] Scientology engineers manufactured a similar
   type of E-meter device, and this began usage in the movement in
   1958.^[87][14]^[88][15] The version of the E-meter developed by Joe
   Wallis and Don Breeding was powered by a battery and was smaller than
   Mathison's device.^[89][16] This device was called the "Hubbard
   electrometer", and was seen as a necessary part of the Scientology
   practice of "[90]Auditing".^[91][16] In 1966, Hubbard received a patent
   in the United States for a "Device for Measuring and Indicating Changes
   in Resistance of a Living Body".^[92][14]^[93][15]
