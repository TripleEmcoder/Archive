   William Harkness

   William Harkness (December 17, 1837 – February 28, 1903) was an
   [7]astronomer, born at [8]Ecclefechan, [9]Scotland on December 17,
   1837, a son of James Harkness (1803–78). He was educated at
   [10]Lafayette College (1854–56), graduated from the [11]University of
   Rochester (1858), and studied [12]medicine in [13]New York City. He
   served as a [14]surgeon in the [15]Union armies during part of the
   [16]American Civil War. From 1862 to 1865 he was an [17]aid in the
   [18]United States Naval Observatory and then, after service on the
   [19]monitor Monadnock [20]{1}(1865–66), was employed in the
   [21]Hydrographic Office.

   During the [22]eclipse of August, 1869, Harkness discovered the
   [23]coronal line K 1474. Three years later he was made a member of the
   [24]Transit of Venus Commission^[25][1], had charge of the party at
   [26]Hobart, Tasmania, in 1879, and at [27]Washington in 1882, when he
   became the executive officer. His most memorable accomplishments are
   related to the construction of [28]telescopes, his theory of the focal
   curve of [29]achromatic telescopes, and on his invention of the
   spherometer caliper, and other [30]astronomical instruments. He was
   astronomical [31]director of the Naval Observatory (1894–99) and
   director of the [32]Nautical Almanac (1897–99). He retired two days
   after attaining the relative rank of [33]rear admiral (December, 1899).
   He was [34]president of the [35]American Association for the
   Advancement of Science (1893). Of his works, The Solar Parallax and its
   Related Constants (1891) is the most important.

   Harkness died in [36]Jersey City on February 28, 1903 at the age of
   66.^[37][2]
