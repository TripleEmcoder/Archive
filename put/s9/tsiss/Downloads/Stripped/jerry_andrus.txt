   Jerry Andrus (January 28, 1918 – August 26, 2007) was an American
   [19]magician and writer known internationally for his original
   [20]close-up, [21]sleight of hand tricks and [22]optical illusions,
   such as the famous "Linking Pins".

[[35]edit] Early life

   Andrus was born January 28, 1918 in [36]Sheridan, Wyoming.^[37][1] At
   the age of 10, he moved to [38]Albany, Oregon, where he lived until his
   death in 2007. At 12, Andrus became interested in the art of illusion
   when he saw a performance of a reformed "[39]spiritual medium".^[40][2]
   He joined the [41]International Society of Junior Magicians when he was
   16 and soon became known as a "magician’s magician".^[42][2]

[[43]edit] Career

[[44]edit] Magic

   A self-taught magician, Andrus preferred to develop his own style
   rather than learn the craft as traditionally handed down from other
   magicians, eventually becoming world renowned as one of the "best and
   most-influential 'close-up magic' performers ever."^[45][1]^[46][3] He
   was known to many accomplished contemporary magicians, such as
   [47]Lance Burton, [48]Doug Henning, and [49]Penn & Teller, for this
   unique brand of close-up, sleight-of-hand magic.^[50][1]

   International [51]card magicians knew Andrus for his "Master Move", a
   sleight-of-hand classic "[52]pass" without "necessary false
   movement".^[53][4]

   An early member of [54]The Magic Castle in [55]Hollywood, California,
   Andrus performed there semi-annually until shortly before his
   death.^[56][1]

[[57]edit] Illusions

   Andrus created his illusions in his Oregon home, which he nicknamed
   "The Castle of Chaos" in reference to the numerous items he collected
   over the years with the hope of using them to "make something
   spectacular".^[58][3]^[59][5]

   In 1954, Andrus created the famous "Linking Pins",^[60][2]^[61][6] a
   close-up illusion in which closed safety pins are rapidly linked
   together in twos, threes and chains.^[62][7]

[[63]edit] Skepticism

   An avowed [64]scientific skeptic and [65]agnostic, Andrus often
   lectured at scientific and skeptic conferences, using his optical
   illusions and magic tricks to demonstrate the ease with which the mind
   can be fooled by the eye. He discussed a form of [66]cognitive science
   that attempted to explain that because the mind is working on an
   unconscious level, it can be fooled into misperceiving apparently
   normal [67]sensory experiences.^[68][1]
