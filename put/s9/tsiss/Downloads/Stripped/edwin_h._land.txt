   Edwin Herbert Land (May 7, 1909 – March 1, 1991) was an [19]American
   [20]scientist and [21]inventor, best known as the co-founder of the
   [22]Polaroid Corporation. Among other things, he invented inexpensive
   filters for [23]polarizing light, a practical system of in-camera
   instant photography, and his [24]retinex theory of [25]color
   [26]vision. His [27]Polaroid [28]instant camera, which went on sale in
   late 1948, made it possible for a picture to be taken and developed in
   60 seconds or less.

[[41]edit] Life and career

[[42]edit] Early years

   Edwin was born in [43]Bridgeport, Connecticut to Harry and Helen Land.
   His father owned a scrap metal yard. He attended the [44]Norwich Free
   Academy at [45]Norwich, Connecticut, a semi-private high school, and
   graduated in the class of 1927. The library there was posthumously
   named for him, having been funded by grants from his family. He studied
   [46]chemistry at [47]Harvard. After his freshman year, he left Harvard
   for New York City.

   In [48]New York City, he invented the first inexpensive filters capable
   of [49]polarizing light, [50]Polaroid film. Because he was not
   associated with an educational institution, he lacked the tools of a
   proper laboratory, making this a difficult endeavor. Instead, he would
   sneak into a laboratory at [51]Columbia University late at night to use
   their equipment.^[52][1] He also availed himself of the New York City
   [53]public library to scour the scientific literature for prior work on
   polarizing substances. His breakthrough came when he realized that
   instead of attempting to grow a large single crystal of a polarizing
   substance, he could manufacture a film with millions of
   micrometre-sized polarizing crystals that were coaxed into perfect
   alignment with each other.

   After developing a polarizing film, Edwin Land returned to Harvard.
   However, he still did not finish his studies or receive a degree. Once
   Land could see the solution to a problem in his head, he lost all
   motivation to write it down or prove his vision to others.^[54][2]
   Often his wife, at the prodding of his instructor, would extract from
   him the answers to homework problems. She would then write up the
   homework and hand it in so he could receive credit and not fail the
   course.

[[55]edit] Land's company

   In 1932 he established the Land-Wheelwright Laboratories together with
   his Harvard physics instructor to commercialize his polarizing
   technology. Wheelwright, his instructor, came from a family of
   financial means and agreed to fund the company. After a few early
   successes developing polarizing filters for sunglasses and photographic
   filters, Land obtained funding from a series of [56]Wall Street
   investors for further expansion. The company was renamed the
   [57]Polaroid Corporation in 1937. Land further developed and produced
   the sheet polarizers under the [58]Polaroid trademark. Although the
   initial major application was for sunglasses and scientific work, it
   quickly found many additional applications: for color animation in the
   [59]Wurlitzer 850 Peacock [60]jukebox of 1942, for glasses in
   full-color stereoscopic (3-D) movies, to control brightness of light
   through a window, a necessary component of all [61]LCDs, and many more.
   During [62]World War II, he worked on military tasks, which included
   developing [63]dark-adaptation goggles, [64]target finders, the first
   passively guided [65]smart bombs, and a special stereoscopic viewing
   system called the [66]Vectograph which revealed camouflaged enemy
   positions in aerial photography.

   A little more than three years later, on February 21, 1947, Edwin Land
   demonstrated an [67]instant camera and associated film. Called the
   [68]Land Camera, it was in commercial sale less than two years later.
   Polaroid originally manufactured sixty units of this first camera.
   Fifty-seven were put up for sale at Boston's [69]Jordan Marsh
   department store before the 1948 Christmas holiday. Polaroid marketers
   incorrectly guessed that the camera and film would remain in stock long
   enough to manufacture a second run based on customer demand. All
   fifty-seven cameras and all of the film were sold on the first day of
   demonstrations.

   During his time at Polaroid, Land was notorious for his marathon
   research sessions. When Land conceived of an idea, he would experiment
   and brainstorm until the problem was solved with no breaks of any kind.
   He needed to have food brought to him and to be reminded to
   eat.^[70][2] He once wore the same clothes for eighteen days straight
   while solving problems with the commercial production of polarizing
   film.^[71][2] As the Polaroid company grew, Land had teams of
   assistants working in shifts at his side. As one team wore out, the
   next team was brought in to continue the work.

[[72]edit] Later years

   In the 1950s, Edwin Land and his team helped design the optics of the
   revolutionary [73]Lockheed U-2 spy plane. Also in this decade, Land
   first discovered a [74]two-color system for projecting the entire
   spectrum of hues with only two colors of projecting light (he later
   found more specifically that one could achieve the same effect using
   very narrow bands of 500 nm and 557 nm light). Some of this work was
   later incorporated in his [75]Retinex theory of [76]color vision. In
   1957, [77]Harvard University awarded him an honorary doctorate, and
   Edwin H. Land Blvd., a street in [78]Cambridge, Massachusetts, was
   later named in his memory. The street forms the beginning of Memorial
   Drive, where the Polaroid company building was located.
   [79]SX-70

   In the early 1970s, Land attempted to explain the previously known
   phenomenon of [80]color constancy with his [81]Retinex theory. His
   popular demonstrations of color constancy raised much interest in the
   concept. He considered his leadership towards the development of
   integral instant [82]color photography — the [83]SX-70 film and camera
   — to be his crowning achievement.

   Although he led the Polaroid Corporation as a chief executive, Land was
   a scientist first and foremost, and as such made sure that he performed
   "an experiment each day". Despite the fact that he held no formal
   degree, employees, friends, and the press respected his scientific
   accomplishments by calling him Dr. Land. The only exception was the
   [84]Wall Street Journal, which refused to use that honorific title
   throughout his lifetime.^[85][2]

   Land often made technical and management decisions based on what he
   felt was right as both a scientist and a humanist, much to the chagrin
   of Wall Street and his investors. From the beginning of his
   professional career, he hired women and trained them to be research
   scientists. Following the assassination of [86]Martin Luther King, Jr.
   in 1968, he led Polaroid to the forefront of the [87]affirmative action
   movement.

   Land had an artistic vision. In his laboratory he built giant studio
   cameras the size of bedroom closets that produced large format (20 x 24
   inch) prints. He gave photographers free access to these cameras in
   return for some of the prints they produced.^[88][3] This practise was
   continued by the company; the result was the Polaroid Collection.
   Compiled since the seventies, the collection grew to between 16,000 and
   24,000 photos shot by some of the world's greatest artists and
   photographers, including [89]Ansel Adams, [90]Chuck Close, [91]Robert
   Frank and [92]Andy Warhol. The collection, an asset of the Polaroid
   Corporation, remained intact till 2010 when in controversial
   circumstances was broken up and put up for sale in lots.^[93][4]

   Despite the tremendous success of his instant cameras, Land's
   unsuccessful [94]Polavision instant movie system was a financial
   disaster, and he resigned as Chairman of Polaroid on March 6, 1980. In
   his retirement years, he founded the [95]Rowland Institute for Science.

[[96]edit] Death

   Land died on March 1, 1991 in [97]Cambridge, Massachusetts, at the age
   of 81. Upon his death, his personal assistant shredded his personal
   papers and notes.

[[98]edit] Public service

   Land was:^[99][5]
     * A member of the President's Science Advisory Committee (PSAC)
       1957–59 and a Consultant-at-Large of PSAC from 1960–1973.
     * A member of the [100]President's Foreign Intelligence Advisory
       Board (PFIAB) 1961–77.
     * A member of the National Commission on Technology, Automation and
       Economic Progress 1964–66.
     * A member of the Carnegie Commission on Educational Television
       1966–67.
     * A Trustee of the Ford Foundation 1967–1975.

[[101]edit] Honors

   Although Land never received a formal degree, he received honorary
   degrees from Harvard, Yale, Columbia, [102]Carnegie Institute of
   Technology, Willams College, [103]Tufts College, Washington University,
   Polytechnic Institute of Brooklyn, University of Massachusetts,
   [104]Brandeis University and many others. He was awarded the
   [105]Presidential Medal of Freedom, the highest award given to a
   [106]U.S. [107]citizen, in 1963 for his work in optics. He held 535
   [108]patents, compared with [109]Thomas Edison's 1,097 American
   patents.^[110][2] In 1967 he was awarded the [111]Frederic Ives Medal
   by the [112]OSA.

   In 1938 he was awarded the [113]Elliott Cresson Medal, the [114]Howard
   N. Potts Medal in 1956, and the [115]Vermilye Medal in 1974, all three
   from [116]The Franklin Institute.

   In 1977 he was inducted into the [117]National Inventors Hall of Fame.
   In 1988 Land was awarded the [118]National Medal of Technology for "the
   invention, development and marketing of instant photography".
