   James Naismith (November 6, 1861 – November 28, 1939) was a
   [19]Canadian and [20]American sports coach and innovator. He invented
   the [21]sport of [22]basketball in 1891 and is often credited with
   introducing the first [23]football helmet. He wrote the [24]original
   basketball rulebook, founded the [25]University of Kansas basketball
   program, and lived to see basketball adopted as an Olympic
   demonstration sport in [26]1904 and as an official event at the
   [27]1936 Summer Olympics in [28]Berlin.

   Born in Ontario, Canada, Naismith studied physical education in
   [29]Montreal before moving to the United States, where he developed
   basketball in late 1891 while enrolled at the International YMCA
   Training School in [30]Springfield, Massachusetts.^[31][1] Naismith
   also studied medicine in Denver, taking his MD in 1898 before moving to
   the University of Kansas. Following a number of years as a faculty
   member and part-time basketball coach—during the sport's fledgling
   years (1898–1907)—at the [32]University of Kansas, Naismith moved on to
   further academic obligations at that university, where he also served
   as the Athletic Director for the [33]Kansas Jayhawks. He became a U.S.
   citizen in 1925.^[34][2]

   Naismith's contributions to basketball have earned him several
   posthumous honours, such as in the [35]Canadian Basketball Hall of
   Fame, the [36]Canadian Olympic Hall of Fame, the [37]Canadian Sports
   Hall of Fame, the Ontario Sports Legends Hall of Fame, the Ottawa
   Sports Hall of Fame, the [38]McGill University Sports Hall of Fame, the
   Kansas State Sports Hall of Fame and the [39]FIBA Hall of Fame. The
   [40]Naismith Memorial Basketball Hall of Fame in in Springfield,
   Massachusetts, was named in Naismith's honour.

[[52]edit] Early years

   Naismith was born in 1861 in Ramsay Township (now [53]Almonte,
   [54]Ontario).^[55][3] Struggling in school but gifted in farm labour,
   Naismith spent his days outside playing catch, hide-and-seek, or
   [56]duck on a rock, a medieval game in which a person guards a large
   [57]drake stone from opposing players, who try to knock it down by
   throwing smaller stones at it. To play duck on a rock most effectively,
   Naismith soon found out that a soft lobbing shot was far more effective
   than a straight hard throw, a thought which later proved essential for
   the invention of [58]basketball.^[59][4] Orphaned early in his life,
   Naismith lived with his aunt and uncle for many years and attended
   grade school at Bennies Corners near Almonte. Then he enrolled in
   Almonte High School, from which he graduated in 1883.^[60][4]

[[61]edit] McGill University

   In the same year, Naismith entered [62]McGill University in
   [63]Montreal. Although described as a slight figure, standing 5 foot 10
   ½ and listed at 168 pounds,^[64][5] he was a talented and versatile
   athlete, representing McGill in [65]Canadian football, [66]soccer and
   [67]gymnastics. He played center on the football team, introduced the
   first football helmet into regular play^[68][4] and won multiple
   Wicksteed medals for outstanding gymnastics performances.^[69][6]
   Naismith earned a BA in Physical Education (1888) and a Diploma at the
   [70]Presbyterian College in Montreal (1890).^[71][4] From 1891 on,
   Naismith taught physical education and became the first McGill director
   of athletics, but then left Montreal to become a [72]physical education
   teacher at the [73]YMCA International Training School in
   [74]Springfield, Massachusetts.^[75][6]

[[76]edit] Springfield College: Invention of "Basket Ball"

   At Springfield YMCA, Naismith struggled with a rowdy class which was
   confined to indoor games throughout the harsh [77]New England winter
   and thus was perpetually short-tempered. Under orders from Dr. Luther
   Gulick, head of Springfield YMCA Physical Education, Naismith was given
   14 days to create an indoor game that would provide an "athletic
   distraction": Gulick demanded that it would not take up much room,
   could help its track athletes to keep in shape^[78][6] and explicitly
   emphasized to "make it fair for all players and not too rough."^[79][5]

   In his attempt to think up a new game, Naismith was guided by three
   main thoughts.^[80][4] Firstly, he analyzed the most popular games of
   those times ([81]rugby, [82]lacrosse, soccer, football, [83]hockey and
   [84]baseball); Naismith noticed the hazards of a small fast ball and
   concluded that the big soft soccer ball was safest. Secondly, he saw
   that most physical contact occurred while running with the ball,
   dribbling or hitting it, so he decided that passing was the only legal
   option. Finally, Naismith further reduced body contact by making the
   goal unguardable, namely placing it high above the player's heads. To
   score goals, he forced the players to throw a soft lobbing shot that
   had proven effective in his old favorite game duck on a rock. Naismith
   christened this new game "Basket Ball"^[85][4] and put his thoughts
   together in [86]13 basic rules.^[87][7]

   The first game of "Basket Ball" was played in December 1891. In a
   handwritten report, Naismith described the circumstances of the
   inaugural match; in contrast to modern [90]basketball, the players
   played nine versus nine, handled a [91]soccer ball, not a basketball,
   and instead of shooting at two hoops, the goals were a pair of peach
   baskets: "When Mr. Stubbins brot [sic] up the peach baskets to the gym
   I secured them on the inside of the railing of the gallery. This was
   about 10 feet from the floor, one at each end of the gymnasium. I then
   put [92]the 13 rules on the bulletin board just behind the instructor's
   platform, secured a soccer ball and awaited the arrival of the class...
   The class did not show much enthusiasm but followed my lead... I then
   explained what they had to do to make goals, tossed the ball up between
   the two center men & tried to keep them somewhat near the rules. Most
   of the fouls were called for running with the ball, though tackling the
   man with the ball was not uncommon."^[93][8] In contrast to modern
   [94]basketball, the original rules did not include what is known today
   as the [95]dribble. Since the ball could only be moved up the court via
   a pass early players tossed the ball over their heads as they ran up
   court. Also, following each "goal" a [96]jump ball was taken in the
   middle of the court. Both practices are obsolete in [97]the rules of
   modern basketball.^[98][9]

   By 1892, basketball had grown so popular on campus that Dennis
   Horkenbach (editor-in-chief of The Triangle, the Springfield college
   newspaper) featured it in an article called "A New Game",^[99][3] and
   there were calls to call this new game "Naismith Ball", but Naismith
   refused.^[100][4] By 1893, basketball was introduced internationally by
   the [101]YMCA movement.^[102][3] From Springfield, Naismith went to
   [103]Denver where he acquired a medical degree and in 1898 he joined
   the [104]University of Kansas faculty at [105]Lawrence,
   Kansas.^[106][5]

[[107]edit] University of Kansas

   The [108]University of Kansas men's basketball program officially began
   in 1898, following Naismith's arrival, just six years after Naismith
   penned the sport's first official rules. Naismith was not initially
   hired to coach basketball, but rather as a chapel director and physical
   education instructor.^[109][10] In these early days, the majority of
   the basketball games were played against nearby [110]YMCA teams, with
   YMCA's across the nation having played an integral part in the birth of
   basketball. Other common opponents were [111]Haskell Indian Nations
   University and [112]William Jewell College. Under Naismith, the team
   played only three current [113]Big 12 schools: Nebraska (six times),
   Missouri (twice), and Kansas State (once). Naismith was, ironically,
   the only coach in the program's history to have a losing record
   (55–60).^[114][11] However, Naismith coached [115]Forrest "Phog" Allen,
   his eventual successor at Kansas,^[116][12] who went on to join his
   mentor in the [117]Basketball Hall of Fame.^[118][13] When Allen became
   a coach himself and told him that he was going to coach basketball at
   [119]Baker University in 1904, Naismith discouraged him: "You can't
   coach basketball; you just play it."^[120][6] Instead, Allen embarked
   on a coaching career that would lead him to be known as "the Father of
   Basketball Coaching." During his time at Kansas, Allen coached
   [121]Dean Smith (1952 National Championship team) and [122]Adolph Rupp
   (1922 Helms Foundation National Championship team). When Dean Smith
   retired as head Basketball coach at North Carolina he was the
   winningest coach in college basketball history, #2 was Adolph Rupp
   (Kentucky) and #3 was Allen. The three coaches have joined Naismith as
   [123]members of the Basketball Hall of Fame.

   By the turn of the century, there were enough college teams in the East
   of the U.S. that the first intercollegiate competitions could be played
   out.^[124][12] Although his sport continuously grew, Naismith long
   regarded his game as a curiosity and preferred gymnastics and
   [125]wrestling as better forms of physical education.^[126][12]
   However, basketball became a [127]demonstration sport at the [128]1904
   Games in [129]St. Louis, USA. As the [130]Basketball Hall of Fame
   reports, Naismith was also neither interested in self-promotion nor in
   the glory of competitive sports.^[131][14] Instead, he was more
   interested in his physical education career, receiving an honorary PE
   Masters degree in 1910,^[132][4] patrolled the [133]Mexican border for
   four months in 1916 during [134]World War I, travelled to France,
   published two books ("A Modern College" in 1911 and "Essence of a
   Healthy Life" in 1918) and took on American citizenship in
   1925.^[135][4]

   In 1935, the [136]National Association of Basketball Coaches (created
   by Naismith's pupil Phog Allen) collected money so that the 74-year old
   Naismith could witness the introduction of basketball into the official
   Olympic sports program of the [137]1936 Summer Olympic Games.^[138][14]
   There, Naismith handed out the medals to three North American teams;
   United States, for the Gold Medal, Canada, for the Silver Medal, and
   [139]Mexico, for their Bronze medal win.^[140][15] During the Olympics,
   he was named the Honorary President of the [141]International
   Basketball Federation.^[142][4] When Naismith returned he commented
   that seeing the game played by many nations was the greatest
   compensation he could have received for his invention.^[143][12] In
   1937, Naismith played a role in the formation of the National
   Association of Intercollegiate Basketball, which later became the
   [144]National Association of Intercollegiate Athletics
   (NAIA).^[145][16]

   In his later years, Naismith became Professor Emeritus in Kansas and
   retired in 1937 at the age of 76. Including his years as coach,
   Naismith served as athletic director and faculty at the school for a
   total of almost 40 years. Naismith died in 1939 after he suffered a
   fatal brain hemorrhage and was buried in [146]Lawrence, Kansas.
   Posthumously, his masterwork "Basketball — its Origins and Development"
   was published in 1941.^[147][4] In Lawrence, Kansas, James Naismith has
   a road named in his honour, Naismith Drive, which runs in front of
   [148]Allen Fieldhouse (the official address of Allen Fieldhouse is 1700
   Naismith Drive), the university's basketball facility. It is a
   separated, four-lane road that runs north and south from University
   Drive south to its end at 24th street, just south of the [149]KU
   campus.^[150][17] The university also named the court in [151]Allen
   Fieldhouse, James Naismith Court in his honour. Naismith Hall, a
   college residential dormitory, is located on the northeastern edge of
   19th Street and Naismith Drive.^[152][17]

[[153]edit] Coaching record

   See also: [154]Kansas Jayhawks men's basketball seasons

   In 1898, Naismith became the first college basketball coach of the
   [155]University of Kansas basketball team. He compiled a record of
   55–60, and ironically became the only Kansas coach to have a losing
   record.^[156][11] Nevertheless, Naismith has one of the greatest
   coaching legacies in basketball history, as he coached [157]Basketball
   Hall of Fame coach [158]Phog Allen, who himself coached Hall of Fame
   coaches [159]Dean Smith, [160]Adolph Rupp, and [161]Ralph Miller at the

[[172]edit] Legacy

   Naismith is the inventor of [173]basketball and has written [174]the
   original 13 rules of this sport.^[175][14] The [176]Naismith Memorial
   Hall of Fame in [177]Springfield, Massachusetts, is named in his
   honour, and he was an inaugural induction in 1959.^[178][14] The
   [179]National Collegiate Athletic Association rewards its best players
   and coaches annually with the [180]Naismith Awards, among them the
   [181]Naismith College Player of the Year, the [182]Naismith College
   Coach of the Year and the [183]Naismith Prep Player of the Year. After
   the Olympic introduction to male athletes in 1936, women's basketball
   became an Olympic event in [184]Montreal during the [185]1976 Summer
   Olympics.^[186][18] Naismith was also inducted into the Canadian
   Basketball Hall of Fame, the [187]Canadian Olympic Hall of Fame, the
   [188]Canadian Sports Hall of Fame, the Ontario Sports Legends Hall of
   Fame, the Ottawa Sports Hall of Fame, the [189]McGill University Sports
   Hall of Fame, the Kansas State Sports Hall of Fame, [190]FIBA Hall of
   Fame, and [191]The Naismith Memorial Basketball Hall of Fame, which was
   named in his honour.^[192][4]

   Basketball is today played by more than 300 million people worldwide,
   making it one of the most popular team sports.^[193][6] In North
   America, basketball has produced some of the most-admired athletes of
   the 20th century. Polls conducted by [194]ESPN and the [195]Associated
   Press named basketball player [196]Michael Jordan respectively first
   and second greatest North American athlete of the 20th century, and
   both polls featured fellow basketballers [197]Wilt Chamberlain (of KU,
   like Naismith) and [198]Bill Russell in the Top 20.^[199][19]^[200][20]

[[201]edit] Personal life

   Naismith was the eldest child of Margaret and John Naismith, two
   Scottish immigrants. His mother, Margaret Young, was born in 1833 and
   immigrated as the fourth of 11 children to [202]Lanark County, Canada
   in 1852.^[203][4] His father, John Naismith, was born in 1836, left
   Europe when he was 18 and also settled down in Lanark County.^[204][4]
   After marrying, John Naismith worked as a saw hand, but unfortunately,
   the couple soon contracted [205]typhoid fever and died when Naismith
   was just 9 years old.^[206][21] He was then raised by a strict,
   religious grandmother and his uncle Peter.^[207][21]^[[208]dead link]

   On June 20, 1894, Naismith married Maude E. Sherman from Springfield.
   The couple had five children: Margaret Mason (1895), Helen Carolyn
   (1897), John Edwin (1900), Maude Ann (1904) and James Sherman
   (1913).^[209][5] He was a member of the [210]Pi Gamma Mu and [211]Sigma
   Phi Epsilon fraternities,^[212][5] and regarding his spiritual beliefs,
   Naismith is remembered as a [213]Freemason.^[214][22] Maude Naismith
   died in 1937, and on June 11, 1939, he married his second wife
   [215]Florence Kincaid. Naismith suffered a major brain hemorrhage on
   November 19 the same year and died nine days later in his home located
   in [216]Lawrence, Kansas.^[217][23] Naismith was 78 years
   old.^[218][21] Naismith is buried with his first wife in Memorial Park
   Cemetery in Lawrence, KS.^[219][24]
