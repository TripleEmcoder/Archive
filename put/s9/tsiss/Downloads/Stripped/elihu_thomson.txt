   Elihu Thomson (March 29, 1853 – March 13, 1937) was an [23]engineer and
   [24]inventor who was instrumental in the founding of major
   [25]electrical companies in the [26]United States, the [27]United
   Kingdom and [28]France.

[[37]edit] Early life

   He was born in [38]Manchester ([39]England) on 29 March 1853, but his
   family moved to [40]Philadelphia in 1858.^[41][1] Thomson attended
   [42]Central High School in Philadelphia and graduated in 1870. Thomson
   took a teaching position at Central, and in 1876, at the age of
   twenty-three, held the Chair of Chemistry. In 1880, he left Central to
   pursue research in the emerging field of electrical
   engineering.^[43][2]

[[44]edit] Electrical innovations

   With [45]Edwin J. Houston, a former teacher and later colleague of
   Thomson's at Central High School, Thomson founded the
   [46]Thomson-Houston Electric Company. Notable inventions created by
   Thomson during this period include an arc-lighting system, an
   automatically regulated three-coil dynamo, a magnetic lightning
   arrester, and a local power transformer.^[47][3] In 1892 the
   [48]Thomson-Houston Electric Company merged with the [49]Edison General
   Electric Company to become the [50]General Electric Company.^[51][4]
   The historian [52]Thomas P. Hughes writes that Thomson "displayed
   methodological characteristics in the workshop and the laboratory as
   [an] inventor and in the business world as [an] entrepreneur. He also
   chose to solve problems in the rapidly expanding field of electric
   light and power."^[53][5] Thomson's name is further commemorated by the
   [54]British Thomson-Houston Company (BTH), and the French companies
   [55]Thomson and [56]Alstom.

   Thomson was notable both for his emphasis on models and for the
   singular focus with which he pursued his research, with Thomson
   referring to his workshop as a "model room" rather than a laboratory.
   Between 1880 and 1885, Thomson averaged twenty-one patent applications
   annually, doubling that average between 1885 and 1890. Upon his
   namesake company's merger to form [57]General Electric, Thomson
   relocated his laboratory to a location near Boston away from GE's
   headquarters to ensure his control over his research.^[58][6] After
   being asked to become the director of GE, Thomson rejected the offer
   preferring continued research to management.

[[59]edit] Honors

   He was the first recipient of the [60]American Institute of Electrical
   Engineers [61]AIEE (now [62]Institute of Electrical and Electronics
   Engineers (IEEE)) [63]Edison Medal, bestowed upon him in 1909 "For
   meritorious achievement in electrical science, engineering and arts as
   exemplified in his contributions thereto during the past thirty years."
   Near the end of his life, Thomson's second wife Clarissa Hovey Thomson
   is reported to have said that she had to carry a basket with her to
   carry all of Thomson's awards and honors.^[64][7]

[[65]edit] Later life

   He was a founding member, as well as the second president, of the
   [66]International Electrotechnical Commission.

   He served as acting president of [67]MIT from 1920-1923.^[68][8]
   Thomson, overcoming his distaste for management accepted this role
   during a critical period for the university when it could not otherwise
   find a president.^[69][9]

   Thomson died at his estate in [70]Swampscott, Massachusetts. The
   [71]Elihu Thomson House in Swampscott was designated a U.S.
   [72]National Historic Landmark in 1976.
