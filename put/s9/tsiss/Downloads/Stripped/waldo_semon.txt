   Waldo Lonsbury Semon (September 10, 1898 – May 26, 1999) was a renowned
   [12]American inventor born in [13]Demopolis, Alabama.^[14][1]

   Semon put his name into the history books for inventing [15]vinyl, the
   world's second most used [16]plastic. He found the [17]formula for
   vinyl by mixing a few synthetic polymers, and the result was a
   substance that was elastic, but wasn't adhesive. Semon worked on
   methods of improving rubber, and eventually developed a synthetic
   substitute. On December 11, 1935, he created Koreosol from salt, coke
   and limestone, a polymer that could be made in any consistency.
   ^[18][2]. Semon made more than 5,000 other synthetic rubber compounds,
   achieving success with Ameripol (AMERican POLymer) in 1940 for the
   [19]B.F. Goodrich company. ^[20][3]. In all, Semon held 116
   [21]patents, and was inducted into the Invention Hall of Fame in 1995
   at age 97.

   Semon is sometimes credited with inventing [22]bubble gum, but this is
   inaccurate. He did invent an indigestible synthetic rubber substance
   that could be used as a bubble gum (and produced exceptionally large
   bubbles), but the product remained a curiosity and was never sold.
   Semon graduated from the [23]University of Washington earning a [24]BS
   in [25]chemistry and a [26]PhD in [27]chemical engineering.

   He was awarded the [28]Elliott Cresson Medal in 1964.
