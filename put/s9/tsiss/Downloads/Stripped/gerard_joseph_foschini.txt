   Gerard Joseph Foschini (born in [20]Jersey City, New Jersey in 1940),
   is an American [21]telecommunications [22]engineer who has worked for
   [23]Bell Laboratories since 1961. His research has covered many kinds
   of data communications, particularly [24]wireless communications and
   [25]optical communications. Foschini has also worked on
   [26]point-to-point systems and [27]networks.

[[28]edit] Biography

   Foschini received the [29]B.S.E.E. [30]degree from the [31]New Jersey
   Institute of Technology, the M.E.E. degree from [32]New York University
   and a [33]Ph.D. from the [34]Stevens Institute of Technology. He has
   also taught at [35]Princeton University and [36]Rutgers University.

   Within the telecommunications engineering field, he is best known for
   his invention of [37]Bell Laboratories Layered Space-Time
   (BLAST).^[38][1]^[39][2]^[40][3] This is a scheme for use in wireless
   communications which recommends the use of multiple [41]antennas at
   both the transmitter and receiver. By careful allocation of the data to
   be transmitted to the transmitting antennas, multiple data streams can
   be transmitted simultaneously within a single [42]frequency band — the
   [43]data capacity of the system then grows directly in line with the
   number of antennas. This represents a significant advance on current,
   single-antenna systems.

   Foschini's 1996 paper^[44][1] (not published until 1998), "On limits of
   wireless communications in a fading environment when using multiple
   antennas", sparked the massive international research effort on
   [45]multiple-input multiple-output wireless systems that continues
   today. Shortly after that publication, in a technical memorandum for
   his employers^[46][2], Foschini introduced the BLAST concept which is
   one of the most widely examined techniques in wireless communications
   research today. Among his later contributions, the paper offering a
   simplified form of the original BLAST architecture, called [47]Vertical
   BLAST (V-BLAST)^[48][3], has also resulted in intensive international
   research efforts. In 2002, Bell Laboratories' patent on BLAST was named
   by [49]MIT's Technology Review Magazine as one of five "Patents to
   watch". According to the [50]American Society for Information Science
   and Technology, Foschini is in the top 0.5% of most widely cited
   authors.^[51][4]

   In 2002, Foschini received the [52]Thomas Alva Edison Patent Award, an
   [53]honor given to [54]New Jersey people who have changed the world
   with their inventions; he has also received the Bell Labs Inventor's
   Award, Gold Award and Teamwork Award and holds the titles of
   "Distinguished Member of Staff, Distinguished Inventor" in the
   Laboratories.^[55][5] Before his work on BLAST, Foschini had already
   been elected a Fellow of the [56]Institute of Electrical and
   Electronics Engineers in 1986, "for contributions to communications
   theory".^[57][6]

   Foschini received the 2008 [58]IEEE Alexander Graham Bell Medal "For
   seminal contributions to the science and technology of multiple-antenna
   wireless communications."
