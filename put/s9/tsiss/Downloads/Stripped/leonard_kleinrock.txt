   Leonard Kleinrock (born June 13, 1934, in [22]New York) is an
   [23]engineer and [24]computer scientist, and a computer science
   professor at [25]UCLA's [26]Henry Samueli School of Engineering and
   Applied Science, who made several important contributions to the field
   of [27]computer networking, in particular to the theoretical side of
   computer networking. He also played an important role in the
   development of the [28]ARPANET, the precursor to the Internet, at
   UCLA.^[29][3]

   His most well-known and significant work is his early work on
   [30]queueing theory, which has applications in many fields, among them
   as a key mathematical background to [31]packet switching, the basic
   technology behind the [32]Internet. His initial contribution to this
   field was his doctoral thesis at [33]Massachusetts Institute of
   Technology in 1962, published in book form in 1964; he later published
   several of the standard works on the subject.

   He has described this work as:

     "Basically, what I did for my PhD research in 1961–1962 was to
     establish a mathematical theory of packet networks...."

   His theoretical work on [34]hierarchical routing, done in the late
   1970s with his then-student [35]Farouk Kamoun, is now critical to the
   operation of today's worldwide Internet.

[[43]edit] ARPANET and the Internet

   In 1969, [44]ARPANET, the world's first packet switched computer
   network, was established on October 29 between nodes at Kleinrock's lab
   at [45]UCLA and [46]Douglas Engelbart's lab at [47]SRI. [48]Interface
   Message Processors (IMP) at both sites served as the [49]backbone of
   the first [50]Internet [51][1].

   In addition to SRI and UCLA, the [52]University of California at Santa
   Barbara, and the [53]University of Utah were part of the original four
   network nodes. By December 5, 1969, the initial 4-node network was
   connected.

   In 1988, Kleinrock was the chairman of a group that presented the
   report Toward a National Research Network to the [54]U.S. Congress
   [55][2]. This report was highly influential and was used to develop the
   [56]High Performance Computing Act of 1991 [57][3], which was
   influential in the development of the Internet as it is known today.
   [58][4] Funding from the bill was used in the development of the 1993
   web browser [59]MOSAIC, at the [60]National Center for Supercomputing
   Applications (NCSA).

[[61]edit] Education and career

   Kleinrock graduated from the noted [62]Bronx High School of Science in
   1951, and received a Bachelor of Electrical Engineering degree in 1957
   from the [63]City College of New York, and a master's degree and a
   doctorate (Ph.D.) in [64]electrical engineering & [65]computer science
   from the [66]Massachusetts Institute of Technology in 1959 and 1963
   respectively. He then joined the faculty at the University of
   California at Los Angeles (UCLA), where he remains to the present day;
   during 1991-1995 he served as the Chairman of the Computer Science
   Department there.

[[67]edit] Awards

   He has received numerous professional awards. Kleinrock was selected to
   receive the prestigious [68]National Medal of Science, the nation's
   highest scientific honor, from President [69]George W Bush in the
   [70]White House on September 29, 2008. "The 2007 National Medal of
   Science to Leonard Kleinrock for his fundamental contributions to the
   mathematical theory of modern data networks, and for the functional
   specification of packet switching, which is the foundation of Internet
   technology. His mentoring of generations of students has led to the
   commercialization of technologies that have transformed the world."
   ^[71][4]
