Chadwick Glenn Jenkins (born May 30, 1979) is a U.S. inventor.

[edit] Biography

Born in Dawson, Georgia, Jenkins was raised in a suburb about 10 miles from downtown Atlanta, Georgia. He had a great interest in business and technology, particularly with Pete Kight's invention of online bill pay; he developed complex software systems such as an audit software at age 22, experimenting in his parents basement. Jenkins attended Harrison High School in Kennesaw, GA where he graduated there in 1997. He received his college education from Kennesaw State University, where he studied accounting, economics, and finance. At college, Jenkins convinced leading corporations to transmit hundreds-of-thousands of records electronically to his proprietary electronic audit software, provided that he met security requirements, and electronically audited billions of dollars in benefit contributions. Not surprisingly, the ability to electronically audit billions of dollars in contributions through an independent auditing solution caught on. Looking to expand, he hit the road in the hope of developing new and improved state-of-the-art solutions for the accounting and finance industries.

After working in various partnerships, and in different fields (including finance, accounting, and technology), in 2004 he secured the rights of the Personal TaxEdge Software trademarks and domains, which were purchased by Intuit Corporation from Bob Parsons for $64 million in 1994.[1] He used the intellectual property to market his 'TaxEdge' (an automated tax management system) for powering tax software. Jenkins began with the provisional patent of "method and system for prepopulating tax returns, but finalized his invention as the "apparatus for management of taxes." Jenkins produced additional patent filings refining the design of his tax management system in the coming years and sold systems to several clients. His program is still used today in online tax software.[2]

In 2007, he established FormFree Holdings Corporation, and began developing risk management, fraud mitigation, and verification systems to address problems which he believed would lead to collapse of the U.S. financial system. His first inventions electronically analyzed loan applicant bank accounts, in real-time, and generated enhanced verification models which lenders would use to accurately predict risk of borrowers.

He has wrote several patents on risk management and credit authorization technologies, based on the notion of enhanced credit applicant verification and real-time access to financial data. He is also a member of the American Bankers Association, Information Systems Audit & Control Association, International Foundation of Employee Benefit Plans, and the Technology Association of Georgia.
